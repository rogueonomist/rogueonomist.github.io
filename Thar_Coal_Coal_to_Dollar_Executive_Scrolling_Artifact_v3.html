Why this matters now

The macro case for Coal to Dollar

Pakistan has already incurred the political, financial, and institutional cost of making Thar bankable. The second phase is therefore not about proving the resource again. It is about converting an existing domestic energy advantage into a broader industrial-policy instrument that reduces reliance on imported energy inputs and drives higher-value production across the country.

Executive framing
1

Falling cost curve

The note emphasizes that mature mine expansion compresses the cost structure. That creates a policy opportunity to distinguish between legacy fully-loaded recovery and merchant coal for priority industrial uses.

2

Rail changes everything

Once Thar is connected to the national network, coal can move as a bulk commodity rather than remain trapped in a pithead generation model.

3

Market design is the bottleneck

Physical rail alone is not enough. The system needs rules for access, slot allocation, quality control, digital traceability, and commercial settlement.

4

Industrial prioritization is essential

Coal-to-Dollar must focus on sectors with measurable foreign-exchange savings, export generation, or energy-system value rather than spreading support indiscriminately.

Four-pillar structure

The policy package

The revised note is anchored around four pillars that must move together. The animation below is designed as a senior-policy-maker overview rather than a technical manual.

Board narrative
I

Common-carrier rail model

Notify the corridor as a strategic freight facility with non-discriminatory access for qualified users. Pakistan Railways remains owner and safety authority, but not the sole commercial mover.

II

Registered Coal Trader regime

Create a formal intermediary layer that can aggregate industrial demand, develop storage infrastructure, and provide dependable coal market access beyond a handful of mine-scale buyers.

III

Pricing architecture

For approved sectors, use an ex-mine policy window based on verified variable cost plus a fixed expansion margin, while keeping rail and terminal charges as separate pass-throughs.

IV

Sector prioritization

Allocate special treatment only where coal produces national value: lower import dependence, stronger exports, or better energy-system resilience.

Open Access Rules

How the corridor should actually work

The revised note is clear: the coal corridor does not require privatization of the railway network. It requires Pakistan Railways to separate infrastructure control from commercial access. The right question is not ownership, but who may move coal under what rules.

Embedding principle

Use Pakistan Railways' emerging track-access pathway and overlay it with a corridor-specific Rail Access Code for coal traffic from Thar.

1. Public network declaration

Pakistan Railways should publish a Network Statement specifying route characteristics, permitted axle loads, terminal capacities, maintenance blocks, path windows, service standards, and access charges for the coal corridor.

2. Standard access agreements

Any eligible party—mine, RCT, approved industrial buyer, or rail operator acting for an eligible principal—should be able to sign a standard Track Access Agreement approved by the Railway Board.

3. Booking and anti-hoarding

Capacity should be allocated through annual baseline requests, monthly nominations, and transparent congestion-management rules. A use-it-or-lose-it clause is essential to prevent slot capture.

4. Charges and service economics

Access pricing should be multi-part: path reservation, variable track-use, terminal charges, demurrage, and performance rebates where service levels are not met.

5. Digital traceability and enforcement

Every movement should be digitally visible: dispatch permit, electronic waybill, wagon-level tonnage record, origin weighbridge certificate, GPS/RFID tracking, and destination discharge confirmation.

Registered Coal Traders

A real market needs real intermediaries

Many industrial users cannot contract directly with mines at the scale, tenor, or logistics complexity needed. RCTs solve that problem by creating an organized downstream coal market, analogous in regulatory logic to licensed intermediaries in Pakistan's fuel sector.

Illustrative licensing ladder

Consultation-draft concept based on the revised note. Thresholds are indicative and intended to show structure.

Core eligibility

Pakistani corporate form, beneficial ownership disclosure, fit-and-proper directors, tax compliance, audited financial statements, and no serious record of fraud or insolvency.

Financial capability

Minimum paid-up capital, net worth, working-capital lines, performance guarantees, insurance, and a credible three-year business and investment plan.

Infrastructure capability

Access to stockyards, depots, rail-connected terminals or unloading arrangements, weighing capability, quality sampling protocols, and HSE readiness.

Operational capability

Named operations and compliance staff, digital stock and dispatch reporting, quality assurance systems, and realistic customer acquisition or demand aggregation plans.

Lifecycle

Application → provisional license → time-bound infrastructure completion → inspection and commissioning → full license → renewal / audit / suspension or cancellation for breach.

Board briefing note

What this policy architecture would make possible

This closing section is not framed as a request for approval. It simply distills the strategic implications of the Coal to Dollar architecture for the Board's consideration and ongoing policy discourse.

Apprising the Board
1. Thar becomes a national industrial input

The central shift is geographic and institutional: coal is no longer confined to mine-mouth power generation and can instead serve multiple industrial nodes across Pakistan.

2. Rail becomes enabling infrastructure

The corridor's value is maximized when it behaves as common-user logistics infrastructure, not as a private bilateral conveyor for a narrow buyer base.

3. A new downstream market can emerge

Registered Coal Traders would provide the aggregation, storage, compliance, and delivery capability needed to make coal commercially usable for dispersed industrial demand.

4. Pricing can become an industrial-policy tool

A variable-cost-plus-expansion-margin framework creates a transparent way to translate mine-scale efficiency gains into downstream competitiveness.

5. Industrial sequencing becomes more disciplined

The framework supports prioritization toward sectors with the clearest import-substitution or export rationale rather than treating all energy-intensive sectors as equally strategic.

6. Imported fuel exposure can decline over time

As domestic coal substitutes for imported energy inputs and feedstocks, the economy's vulnerability to external fuel-price shocks can fall meaningfully.

7. The corridor can support both power and non-power use

Coal moved through an open-access system can simultaneously strengthen power-sector fuel security and broaden industrial utilization beyond the electricity market.

8. The Board already sits near the policy center of gravity

Because pricing, project treatment, and the broader Thar ecosystem already converge around existing institutions, much of the architecture can be built by extending current authority rather than inventing a wholly new system.
