Module Overview

What is a Eurobond — and Why Does Pakistan's Yield Matter?

On April 17, 2026, Pakistan priced a new US dollar-denominated Eurobond at a yield of 6.95%, maturing in April 2029 — its first fresh sovereign Eurobond issuance after a nearly five-year hiatus from global capital markets. This transaction sits at the intersection of sovereign credit, interest rate benchmarks, and investor risk appetite. Understanding why Pakistan pays 6.95% — and not 3.95% or 9.95% — is the central question of this module.

Key Concepts

Building Blocks: What You Need to Know First

Eurobond
A bond issued in a currency other than the issuer's home currency, sold internationally across multiple markets. Pakistan's Eurobonds are USD-denominated, issued under English law, and settle through Euroclear/Clearstream. The "Euro" prefix is historical — it has nothing to do with the euro currency.
Yield to Maturity (YTM)
The total annualised return an investor earns if they hold the bond to maturity, assuming all coupon payments are reinvested at the same rate. When we say Pakistan "priced at 6.95%," we mean the YTM at issuance equals 6.95%. If the coupon is also set at 6.95%, the bond is issued at par (100 cents).
Risk-Free Rate
The return on an asset assumed to carry zero default risk. For USD-denominated sovereign bonds, the benchmark is US Treasury securities. The 3-year US Treasury yielded ~3.75% on the Pakistan pricing date (April 17, 2026). Every other sovereign is priced as a spread above this baseline.
Credit Spread
The additional yield an investor demands over the risk-free rate to compensate for default probability, liquidity constraints, and market risk. Pakistan's spread of ~320 bps means investors require 3.20 percentage points over US Treasuries to lend to the Government of Pakistan.
Sovereign Credit Rating
An assessment by agencies (Moody's, Fitch, S&P) of a government's willingness and ability to service its debt. Ratings range from AAA (near-zero default risk) to C/D (default). Pakistan currently holds Caa1 (Moody's) and B+ (Fitch) — both sub-investment grade, meaning the bond is "high yield" or "speculative grade."
Price vs. Yield Inverse Relationship
Bond prices and yields move in opposite directions. If a $1,000 bond with a 6.95% coupon can be bought for $950 in the secondary market, its yield is higher than 6.95%. When credit perception improves, prices rise and yields compress — and vice versa.
Context

Pakistan's Return to Global Capital Markets

Pakistan last issued a sovereign Eurobond in April 2021, raising $2.5 billion across three tranches (5-year at 6%, 10-year at 7.375%, 30-year at 8.875%). A combination of balance-of-payments stress, near-default conditions in 2023, and multiple IMF programme reviews kept it out of commercial markets for five years. The April 2026 issuance signals a turning point — underwritten by rating upgrades, $20+ billion in foreign reserves, and the demonstration of timely Eurobond repayment on April 8, 2026 ($1.3 billion principal + $126 million coupons).

Why This Matters Pedagogically
Pakistan's Eurobond is textbook sovereign risk in action: a sub-investment-grade emerging market with improving-but-fragile fundamentals, re-entering global markets after distress, paying a yield that precisely reflects the market's probability-weighted assessment of its creditworthiness. Every component of the 6.95% yield — the risk-free rate, the credit premium, the liquidity premium — can be dissected and taught.
Core Methodology

How Sovereign Bond Pricing Works

Sovereign bond pricing is anchored in present value theory. A bond's price equals the sum of discounted future cash flows, where the discount rate reflects the risk-adjusted required return of investors. In practice, new sovereign bonds are priced through a bookbuilding process anchored to a benchmark yield plus a negotiated credit spread.

The Fundamental Identity
Bond Pricing Formula
P = Σ [C / (1 + y)ᵗ] + [F / (1 + y)ⁿ]
P = Price  ·  C = Coupon Payment  ·  y = Yield per period  ·  t = Period  ·  F = Face Value  ·  n = Total Periods
Sovereign Yield Decomposition
Sovereign Yield = Risk-Free Rate + Credit Spread
Credit Spread = Default Risk Premium + Liquidity Premium + Country Risk Premium
Pakistan Example: 6.95% = 3.75% (UST 3yr) + 3.20% (credit spread)
Issuance Process

From Road Show to Settlement: How a Sovereign Eurobond is Issued

1
Mandate & Documentation
The government mandates 3–5 lead managers (global investment banks: e.g., Citi, JPMorgan, Deutsche Bank). A prospectus is prepared under the sovereign's Global Medium-Term Note (GMTN) programme, covering financial terms, legal jurisdiction, use of proceeds, and risk disclosures. Pakistan's GMTN programme is listed on the London Stock Exchange.
2
Investor Road Show
Finance Ministry officials and lead managers travel to major financial centres (London, New York, Hong Kong, Dubai, Zurich) to present the sovereign's economic case to institutional investors — pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, hedge funds, asset managers. The road show typically lasts 3–5 days. Investor feedback begins to shape pricing expectations.
3
Initial Price Talk (IPT)
Lead managers publish an Initial Price Talk — a yield range (e.g., "7.25% area") to gauge market demand. This is not a commitment; it is an anchor for bookbuilding. Investors submit "orders" — the size they would buy at what yield.
4
Bookbuilding & Pricing
As the order book builds, lead managers tighten guidance. If demand is strong (book is "oversubscribed"), the yield is revised tighter (lower spread over benchmark = better terms for the issuer). Pakistan's 2021 issuance was reportedly 3–4x oversubscribed; its 2026 issuance benefited from improved ratings and a demonstrated repayment track record. Final pricing is set at the yield that clears the market.
5
Allocation & Settlement
The government, with advice from lead managers, allocates the bond across investor types. Allocation decisions can be strategic — giving priority to long-term real-money investors (pension funds, insurers) over short-term hedge funds ensures better secondary market performance. Settlement typically occurs T+5 through Euroclear. The government receives proceeds minus fees (typically 0.25–0.50% of issuance).
6
Secondary Market Trading
After settlement, the bond trades freely in the over-the-counter (OTC) secondary market, via Bloomberg and Reuters electronic platforms. Prices move daily based on: changes in US Treasury rates (which shift the benchmark), changes in the sovereign's perceived creditworthiness, and global risk appetite (when investors "flee to safety," EM spreads widen).

Spread Dynamics

What Moves Sovereign Spreads?

📉
Spread-Tightening Drivers
Factors that reduce the credit premium
  • IMF programme on-track; tranche disbursements on schedule
  • Rating upgrade by Moody's, Fitch, or S&P
  • FX reserves building above import cover thresholds
  • Current account deficit narrowing
  • Strong Eurobond repayment (demonstrated July–April 2026)
  • Global risk-on sentiment (EM bonds rally)
📈
Spread-Widening Drivers
Factors that increase the credit premium
  • IMF review delay or programme breakdown
  • Geopolitical escalation (India-Pakistan tensions)
  • FX reserve depletion; bilateral deposit non-rollover
  • Fiscal slippage: higher deficit than IMF targets
  • Political instability: government collapse, military intervention
  • Global risk-off (US recession fears, Fed rate hikes)
⚖️
External Benchmark Drivers
UST rate changes flow through to all spreads
  • Fed rate decisions directly shift the 3-year UST anchor
  • US inflation data (CPI, PCE) reprices the real yield component
  • Global oil price shocks (Iran conflict: raised US CPI to 3.3% in Mar 2026)
  • USD strength reduces EM bond attractiveness to local investors
  • Correlated sell-offs: EM spreads tend to widen together in crises

The three major agencies (Moody's, Fitch, S&P) each maintain their own scales, but the correspondence is close enough to be mapped. This creates a trading opportunity: if Moody's upgrades (likely given the trajectory), spreads could tighten meaningfully as Caa1-constrained institutional investors gain access.
Analytical Framework

What Drives Sovereign Risk? Quantitative & Qualitative Factors

Sovereign credit assessment is more art than science — it blends hard macro-financial indicators with nuanced political and institutional judgements. Rating agencies, bond analysts, and investors all use variants of the same framework. The goal is to estimate a government's ability to pay (fiscal and external solvency) and its willingness to pay (political economy, history, institutional quality).

The Two Axes of Sovereign Risk: Unlike corporate credit, sovereign default is almost never pure inability — governments can technically inflate, tax, or cut spending to service debt. Default is almost always a political choice: the government decides that the cost of adjustment exceeds the cost of default. This makes political and institutional factors uniquely important in sovereign credit analysis.
Quantitative Factors

Measurable Indicators of Debt Sustainability & External Viability

📊
Debt Sustainability Metrics
Is the debt stock manageable?
  • Debt/GDP ratio: Pakistan ~85%; BBB threshold ~50–60%; AAA threshold <30%. High but not unique in EM. IMF's debt sustainability analysis sets maximum thresholds.
  • Interest/Revenue ratio: Pakistan spends ~60% of revenues on debt servicing — one of the highest globally. Even minor yield increases are material.
  • Debt maturity profile: Concentration of near-term maturities (as seen April 2026) creates rollover risk. A smooth amortisation schedule reduces refinancing pressures.
  • Fiscal deficit: Pakistan ~7.7% of GDP (FY25); IMF target ~3.5% over programme. Structural primary deficits are unsustainable without growth above interest rates.
💱
External Sector Metrics
Can the country earn the FX to service USD debt?
  • FX reserves / import cover: Pakistan ~3.1 months (Apr 2026). IMF benchmark: 3 months. Below this signals vulnerability. 12 months would be ideal for a B-rated credit.
  • Current account deficit: Pakistan's CAD has narrowed sharply under IMF adjustment, but structural trade deficit remains. Export-to-GDP ratio is very low at ~10%.
  • External debt / GDP and external debt service: Pakistan's external debt ~35% of GDP. Upcoming lumpy repayments (CPEC, bilateral deposits) amplify near-term risk.
  • Remittance reliance: Pakistan receives ~$30B/year in remittances (~8% of GDP) — a critical and relatively stable FX inflow that buttresses reserves but creates an "FX dependency" vulnerability.
📈
Growth & Inflation Metrics
The economic foundation for debt service capacity
  • Real GDP growth vs. real interest rate: When growth (g) > real interest rate (r), debt/GDP ratio naturally falls without additional fiscal tightening. Pakistan's challenge: r has historically exceeded g.
  • Inflation: Elevated inflation (Pakistan CPI peaked at 38% in 2023) erodes real wages, reduces domestic savings, and undermines currency stability — all negative for sovereign credit.
  • GDP per capita: Pakistan's ~$1,500 GDP per capita limits tax base and adjustment capacity relative to higher-income EM peers at similar debt ratios.
  • Current account balance as % of GDP: A chronic deficit must be financed with capital inflows; loss of market access turns a CAD into a crisis trigger.
🏦
Banking Sector & Market Indicators
Financial system health and market signals
  • Sovereign CDS spread: Credit Default Swap spreads provide real-time market pricing of default probability, often leading rating agency actions by weeks or months.
  • Local currency bond yields: Pakistan's PKRV 10-year at ~12.7% (Apr 2026) reflects domestic inflation expectations and SBP policy outlook.
  • FX rate and volatility: Sharp currency depreciation signals reserve pressure and forces imported inflation — a negative feedback loop. PKR/USD is a leading indicator.
  • Banking sector exposure to sovereign debt: Pakistan's banks hold ~70%+ of government securities — a "sovereign-bank nexus" where fiscal stress immediately impairs the banking system's capital.
Qualitative Factors

The "Softer" Dimensions That Often Drive the Hard Numbers

🏛️
Political & Governance Risk
Willingness to pay; institutional capacity to adjust
  • Political continuity and stability: Governments with strong mandates can implement painful fiscal reforms. Fragile coalitions signal reform fatigue and possible programme breakdown.
  • Institutional quality: Rule of law, property rights, bureaucratic competence, and corruption levels all affect the government's ability to collect taxes and control spending.
  • Civil-military relations: In Pakistan's context, the military's economic footprint and interference in fiscal policy is a key qualitative risk factor flagged by rating agencies.
  • Judicial independence: Courts that block government revenue measures (e.g., tax amendments) directly impair fiscal adjustment capacity.
  • Default history: Pakistan has never technically defaulted on external bonds — a critical qualitative positive that distinguishes it from Sri Lanka, Zambia, or Argentina.
🤝
International Relations & Geopolitical Risk
Friends in need — and the risks that come with them
  • IMF programme status: For Pakistan, the IMF's EFF ($7B) is the anchor of external confidence. On-track reviews signal policy credibility; delayed reviews trigger spread widening.
  • Bilateral partner support: Saudi Arabia, UAE, China, and Qatar provide deposit rollovers and balance-of-payments support that effectively "top up" reserves — but this is liability-creating, not grant-based.
  • Regional security environment: The April 2026 India-Pakistan-Iran nexus (US-Iran negotiations hosted in Islamabad) illustrates how geopolitical centrality can be a double-edged asset.
  • Sanctions risk: Financial sanctions (as imposed on Russia, Iran) can restrict market access. Pakistan's FATF "grey list" status in 2018–2022 demonstrated how regulatory stigma affects borrowing costs.
📝
Policy Framework & Credibility
Can the government follow through?
  • Monetary policy independence: An independent central bank with a credible inflation target narrows the "inflation tax" risk on domestic debt and supports currency stability.
  • Tax policy and revenue effort: Pakistan's tax-to-GDP ratio of ~9–10% is structurally insufficient for debt sustainability at 85% debt/GDP. FBR reform progress is a key qualitative rating driver.
  • Subsidy and SOE reform: Energy-sector circular debt and SoE losses impose quasi-fiscal costs that inflate the true deficit beyond headline numbers.
  • Track record of reform implementation: Pakistan's history of IMF programme abandonment (9 out of ~24 programmes were abandoned) is a recurring qualitative concern that keeps spreads elevated relative to pure fundamentals.
🌏
Structural & Long-Run Factors
Determinants of economic resilience over a 5–10 year horizon
  • Economic diversification: Pakistan's over-reliance on textiles and remittances limits export diversification and value-added potential — reducing long-run growth capacity.
  • Demographic dividend: A young, large population can be an asset (future workforce) or a liability (unemployment, urbanisation stress) depending on human capital investment.
  • Climate vulnerability: The 2022 floods destroyed 4% of GDP in one event. Pakistan is among the world's most climate-vulnerable sovereigns — an increasingly material credit factor.
  • Investor base diversification: A reliance on a narrow investor base (GCC sovereign wealth, a few EM-focused funds) amplifies spread volatility when that base turns over.
Synthesising Qualitative and Quantitative
Rating agencies typically begin with a quantitative anchor — a country's fiscal and external metrics — then apply qualitative notch adjustments. Moody's, for example, assesses Pakistan's economic strength at Baa3 level (decent size and resilience) but notches it down multiple times for very weak fiscal strength, high susceptibility to event risk, and fragile governance. The result: Caa1. The gap between what the economy could support and what governance delivers is the core Pakistan credit story.

Key Takeaway: What Pakistan's 6.95% Tells Us
Pakistan's pricing at 6.95% — tighter than Nigeria and Kenya, broadly in line with Egypt — reflects a market that is giving Pakistan meaningful credit for: (a) its IMF-anchored macro stabilisation, (b) three consecutive rating upgrades, (c) the demonstrated repayment of $1.4 billion in Eurobond obligations on April 8, 2026, and (d) the broader geopolitical support network from Saudi Arabia, UAE, and China. The spread of 320 bps is not cheap — it embeds Pakistan's structural vulnerabilities (high interest/revenue ratio, reserve fragility, political uncertainty) — but it is not distressed. It is the yield of a country in managed transition: not yet investment grade, but no longer flirting with default.
