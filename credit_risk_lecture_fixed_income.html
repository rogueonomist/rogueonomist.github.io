Section I

The Credit Risk Assessment Framework

Credit risk is the probability that a borrower fails to meet its contractual obligations — whether in interest, principal, or both. In fixed income analysis, the analyst's job is to answer one fundamental question: given the promised cash flows, is the offered spread compensation sufficient for the risk carried? What follows is a practitioner's framework, structured around five domains.

The 5C Framework — Modified for Fixed Income

The classic 5C framework (Character, Capacity, Capital, Collateral, Conditions) was developed for bank lending but remains the most durable mental model in credit analysis. For fixed income instruments, each dimension carries specific analytical depth.

1 — Character

Governance & Track Record

✓
Management pedigree and sector experience. Who runs the company and what is their track record on debt repayment?
✓
Board composition and independence. Are there independent directors? Any governance red flags?
✓
Related party transactions. Are significant revenues or costs routed through connected entities?
✓
Regulatory compliance history. Any defaults, reschedulings, or disputes with lenders?
✓
For sukuk: Shariah structure quality — is it substance-over-form or purely documentary compliance?

2 — Capacity

Cash Flow Generation

✓
Operating cash flow coverage of debt service: the single most important metric. EBITDA or CFO coverage of interest + principal due.
✓
Revenue quality: recurring vs. one-off, diversified vs. concentrated. Government-contracted revenue is higher quality than commercial.
✓
Margin trajectory: expanding or compressing? Is cost inflation outpacing pricing power?
✓
Free cash flow = Operating Cash Flow − Maintenance Capex. This is the real number that services debt.
✓
Working capital cycle: Days Receivable + Days Inventory − Days Payable. A worsening cycle can create a cash squeeze even in profitable companies.

3 — Capital

Balance Sheet Strength

✓
Leverage ratios: Total Debt/Equity, Net Debt/EBITDA. For short-term instruments, the liquidity position matters more than long-term solvency.
✓
Equity buffer: Does the company have sufficient equity to absorb losses before defaulting on debt?
✓
Debt maturity profile: Is there a refinancing cliff? Are bullet maturities concentrated?
✓
Contingent liabilities: Guarantees, off-balance-sheet items, pension obligations, or litigation.
✓
Asset tangibility: Hard assets (PPE, land) are more defensible collateral than receivables or intangibles.

4 — Collateral

Security Package & Recovery

✓
Secured vs. unsecured: Secured debt recovers more in default. The nature of collateral determines recovery rates — property and plant are superior to receivables which are superior to biological assets or inventory.
✓
Ranking: Is this a first-ranking charge or a pari passu arrangement with other creditors?
✓
Margin/coverage: What is the collateral-to-exposure ratio? A 25% margin means PKR 1.25 of collateral backs PKR 1.00 of debt — this is the minimum standard, not comfortable.
✓
Enforcement risk: How quickly can the security be enforced under Pakistan's legal framework? Receivables are easier to enforce than land, which has legal and time delays.
✓
Structural enhancements: Escrow accounts, Debt Payment Accounts (DPA), collection routing, and lien arrangements can significantly strengthen an otherwise weak security package.

5 — Conditions

Macro, Sector & Deal Terms

✓
Macro environment: Interest rate trajectory affects both the company's cost of debt and the investor's reinvestment yield. In Pakistan, rate normalization post-2023 peak is a key factor.
✓
Sector risk: Each sector has unique risk drivers — regulatory pricing risk (OMCs), commodity risk (dairy), government contract dependency (transport).
✓
Deal structure: Bullet vs. amortizing? Working capital vs. capex? Amortizing is lower risk as it reduces exposure over time.
✓
Covenant package: Are there financial maintenance covenants (ICR floors, leverage caps) and/or affirmative covenants (reporting, lien restrictions)?
✓
Rating agency perspective: What is the difference between entity rating (long-term creditworthiness) and instrument rating (this specific deal)?

Red Flags in Credit Analysis

Red flags are signals that warrant deeper investigation or higher required spreads. No single red flag is necessarily disqualifying — but multiple red flags in the same transaction should raise the alarm significantly.

Financial Red Flags
  • ▲ Receivables growing faster than revenue — possible aggressive revenue recognition
  • ▲ Negative free cash flow despite positive reported earnings
  • ▲ Declining ICR or DSCR trend over 3+ periods
  • ▲ Short-term debt funding long-term assets — maturity mismatch
  • ▲ Revenue concentrated in unrealized/forecasted segments
  • ▲ Bullet repayment on working capital facility — rollover risk
  • ▲ Working capital chronically negative or worsening
  • ▲ High leverage (Net Debt/EBITDA > 4x) for cyclical industries
Structural & Governance Red Flags
  • ▲ Unsecured instrument in a capital-intensive company — high loss-given-default
  • ▲ First-time credit rating — no rating agency track record
  • ▲ Concentrated ownership with weak independent board presence
  • ▲ Proceeds used for "general working capital" without specific deployment plan
  • ▲ Weak covenant package — no ICR maintenance covenants
  • ▲ Related party revenues forming large share of total revenue
  • ▲ Group-level complexity that obscures consolidated exposure
  • ▲ Biological assets, intangibles, or hard-to-value items as primary collateral

Duration, Spread, and Pricing — The Quantitative Side

For short-term instruments (6–12 months), interest rate risk is minimal. Modified Duration for a 6-month bullet instrument is approximately:

MD = (Macaulay Duration) / (1 + YTM/k) ≈ 0.47 years

This means a 100 bps parallel shift in KIBOR changes the bond price by approximately 0.47%. The DV01 (price change per 1 bps move) on a PKR 1,000,000 certificate at ~12% YTM is roughly PKR 47. Duration risk is therefore not the primary concern here — credit risk dominates.

For the amortizing instrument (At-Tahur), weighted average duration is higher — approximately 0.72–0.75 years — reflecting the longer weighted average life of principal outstanding.

The credit spread (KIBOR + X bps) can be decomposed into three components for analytical clarity:

ComponentDescriptionTypical Range (ST PKR)
Credit Risk PremiumCompensation for probability of default × loss given default50–100 bps
Liquidity PremiumPrivate placement vs. listed, thin secondary market50–100 bps
Structure PremiumUnsecured (+), bullet (+), weak covenants (+)0–100 bps
Total Spread (A1)Sum of components for a short-term A1 sukuk100–300 bps

Pakistan-specific context: The SBP policy rate peaked at 22% in June 2023 and was cut to 12% by January 2025, with further easing to ~11% by early 2026. The 6M KIBOR reflects this easing — at ~11.05% in Oct 2025 vs. ~23% at peak. The fall in base rates dramatically compressed the all-in cost of capital, making capital market sukuk more attractive versus bank financing.

Section II

The Credit Analyst's Ratio Toolkit

Ratios are shortcuts — they compress large amounts of financial information into a single number that can be compared across time and peers. The following tables provide formula, interpretation, and investment-grade benchmarks for each key ratio. Benchmarks should be treated as starting points, not absolute rules — sector context matters enormously.

Coverage Ratios — Can They Service the Debt?

Coverage ratios measure whether operating earnings are sufficient to cover the cost of debt. These are the most directly relevant ratios for assessing short-term default probability.

Ratio Formula Minimum (IG) Comfortable (IG) Interpretation
Interest Coverage Ratio (ICR) EBIT / Finance Cost 1.5× 3×+ Core measure of capacity. Below 1.5× is distress territory. Note: some analysts use EBITDA for capital-intensive sectors.
Debt Service Coverage (DSCR) (EBITDA − Tax − Capex) / (Interest + Principal) 1.2× 2×+ Stricter than ICR as it includes principal repayment. Most relevant for amortizing structures. A DSCR below 1.0× means the company cannot service debt from operations alone.
Cash Interest Coverage Operating Cash Flow / Cash Finance Paid 1.5× 2.5×+ Uses actual cash flows rather than accrual earnings. More conservative and less susceptible to accounting manipulation.
Fixed Charge Coverage (EBIT + Lease Pmts) / (Interest + Lease Pmts) 1.5× 2.5×+ Important for companies with significant operating lease obligations (e.g., transport companies with leased vehicles and terminals).

Key nuance for Pakistan: With policy rates at 22% in 2023, many companies saw ICRs collapse. The 2024–2026 rate normalization cycle has significantly improved coverage ratios. When benchmarking, always contextualise against the rate environment — an ICR of 2.5× at 22% rates implies far stronger underlying earnings power than the same ratio at 11% rates.

Leverage Ratios — How Indebted Is the Company?

Leverage ratios assess the magnitude of debt relative to the company's earnings capacity and equity buffer. Higher leverage means less cushion for creditors in a downturn.

Ratio Formula Caution Zone Comfortable (IG) Interpretation
Net Debt / EBITDA (Total Debt − Cash) / EBITDA 3–4× <2.5× The gold standard leverage metric. Measures how many years of operating profit it would take to repay all net debt. Below 2.5× is generally investment grade. Cyclical sectors need lower tolerance.
Total Debt / Equity (Gearing) Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity 1.5–2× <1× Measures financial leverage. Asset-heavy businesses (infrastructure, utilities) tolerate higher gearing. Service businesses should be closer to 0.5×.
Total Debt / Total Assets Total Debt / Total Assets 50–60% <40% Asset-funded perspective. Tells you what fraction of assets are financed by debt — creditor's equity cushion in liquidation.
Long-term Debt / Equity LT Debt / Equity 0.8–1.2× <0.5× Isolates structural (long-term) leverage from short-term financing. Short-term working capital facilities distort total D/E.

Leverage alert: For short-term (6-month) sukuk specifically, the relevant leverage question is NOT the long-term capital structure — it's the current liquidity position. A company with high long-term leverage but strong short-term liquidity (cash, receivables, revolving lines) can comfortably repay a 6-month bullet. This is why you must always look at Current Ratio alongside leverage.

Liquidity Ratios — Can They Repay in the Short Term?

For short-duration instruments, liquidity analysis is often more important than long-term solvency. A company may be fundamentally solvent but illiquid at the point of repayment.

Ratio Formula Minimum Comfortable Interpretation
Current Ratio Current Assets / Current Liabilities 1.0× 1.5×+ Basic liquidity test. Below 1.0× means current liabilities exceed short-term assets — immediate liquidity stress. Note: current assets include receivables and inventory which may not be immediately liquid.
Quick Ratio (Cash + Receivables) / Current Liabilities 0.7× 1.0×+ Strips out inventory. More conservative — measures ability to pay from near-liquid assets. Critical for companies with large inventory holdings.
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) Inventory Days + Receivable Days − Payable Days >60 days <30 days Measures how efficiently the company converts investments in inventory/receivables into cash. A shorter (or negative) CCC means less working capital is trapped in operations.
Net Working Capital (NWC) Current Assets − Current Liabilities Negative Positive The absolute cushion of short-term assets above obligations. Negative NWC signals potential rollover risk for current debt maturities.

Receivables quality: Large receivable balances (especially from government entities in Pakistan) require scrutiny. Government-owed receivables can take months to collect, and in some sectors (power, public transport) they are subject to circular debt dynamics. Always ask: Are receivables from creditworthy, diversified counterparties? What is the average collection period?

Profitability — The Engine That Services Debt

Profitability ratios matter in credit analysis because they determine the cushion available to absorb shocks before debt service is impaired. However, accounting profits can be manipulated — always triangulate with cash flow metrics.

Ratio Formula Industry Benchmark Red Flag Interpretation
Gross Margin Gross Profit / Revenue Sector-specific Declining Reflects pricing power and direct cost control. Commoditized sectors (OMCs: ~4%) vs. value-added (dairy: ~24%). Compression signals competitive pressure or cost inflation.
EBITDA Margin EBITDA / Revenue Sector-specific <5% Operating efficiency metric. Very thin EBITDA margins (below 5%) mean small revenue shocks translate immediately to debt coverage problems.
Net Margin PAT / Revenue Sector-specific <2% Bottom-line profitability after all charges including finance cost and taxes. Watch for finance cost distortion — a company with a 1.5% net margin but 5% EBITDA margin is heavily penalized by debt costs.
Return on Equity (ROE) PAT / Shareholders' Equity 15–20% <8% For credit analysts, ROE matters because it signals management's ability to generate returns — companies with sustainable ROE above cost of equity are healthier credits.
Asset Turnover Revenue / Total Assets Sector-specific Declining Efficiency of asset utilization. Declining asset turnover with flat revenue signals asset bloat — a risk factor in working capital intensive businesses.

Character Assessment: Business Profile

DPEBSL is a 27-year-old transport and logistics conglomerate — one of the most recognizable brands in Pakistan's intercity travel. Originally established in 1997 by GM Korea, it was acquired by Asia Pak Investments in 2011 and has since transformed from a pure bus operator into a multi-segment transport, logistics, and waste management group.

The company's ultimate controlling shareholder is Mr. Sheharyar Arshad Chishty — a former Citigroup/Nomura investment banker who advised on USD 60 billion of M&A transactions. This caliber of sponsorship is a strong positive signal on governance and financial sophistication.

Crucially, the same group controls K-Electric (66.4% via KES Power → IGCF SPV → K-Power Holdings → Sage Ventures → Asia Pak). This group-level linkage is a double-edged sword: it represents scale and resources, but also concentration risk and potential related-party complications.

For a 6-month sukuk being repaid in June 2026, the question is not CY25 annual revenue — it is whether cash flows in H1 2026 are sufficient for bullet repayment. The O&M contracts provide a reliable anchor.

Receivables spike — requires explanation: Receivables jumped from PKR 2,410 Mn (CY23) to PKR 5,850 Mn (CY24) — a 143% increase while revenue grew only 12%. This is the single most significant analytical flag in the DPEBSL financials. The Unsecured Problem: This is the most critical structural weakness. In a default scenario, the sukuk holders would rank pari passu with other unsecured creditors — with no preferential claim on specific assets. Given DPEBSL's asset base includes PPE of PKR 15.8Bn (CY24), the company is not asset-poor. Spread Adequacy Assessment

The 250 bps spread is the widest of the three transactions. Is it adequate compensation for the unsecured, bullet structure on an A1-rated issuer? The analysis below frames this question through expected value:

PROBABILITY OF DEFAULT (1-year, A1 short-term)

For an A1-rated entity globally, 1-year PD is approximately 0.05–0.10%. However, Pakistan's sovereign rating (B3/CCC+) and general credit environment imply a local-market calibration. A reasonable analyst estimate for A-rated Pakistani corporates is 0.50–1.50% (1-year PD).

LOSS GIVEN DEFAULT (LGD) — UNSECURED

Unsecured creditors in Pakistani corporate restructurings typically recover 20–40% (LGD of 60–80%). For DPEBSL, the strong asset base (PPE PKR 15.8Bn) suggests recovery could be above average — perhaps 40–50% (LGD 50–60%).

Expected Loss = PD × LGD (6 months, half-year adjustment)
EL = 0.75% (mid PD) × 55% (mid LGD) × 0.5 (6M) = 0.21% or ~21 bps annualized. The 250 bps spread provides approximately 11.9× coverage of expected loss. Even applying a 3× buffer for uncertainty, the spread appears adequate, though the unsecured structure means investors bear the full severity risk in a tail scenario.

Investment Verdict — DPEBSL ST Sukuk
For a QIB investor with short-term PKR deployment needs, DPEBSL represents an acceptable credit risk at 6MK + 250 bps. The A1 instrument rating, strong brand franchise, and government O&M revenue anchor provide reasonable comfort for a 6-month horizon. The 250 bps spread over KIBOR adequately compensates for the unsecured structure and the construction risk in the waste management segment. The primary risk is not fundamental insolvency but rather a liquidity squeeze at bullet maturity — unlikely but not dismissible given the receivables concentration. Condition for comfort: confirmation that the receivables spike reflects collectible government O&M payments, not disputed claims.

Business Profile — Pakistan's Premium Dairy Play

At-Tahur Limited, trading under the PREMA brand, is a vertically integrated dairy operation — from Australian and Dutch herd to consumer packaging — based in District Kasur, Punjab. Founded in 2007, it represents one of the few institutionally run dairy farms in a sector dominated by small-holder producers.

The company's value proposition is traceability: a direct farm-to-table model where milk is processed at an on-site facility, eliminating third-party aggregation risks. This is strategically important as Pakistan's packaged milk penetration remains at only ~8.5% of total consumption — suggesting a long runway for branded packaged dairy.

PSX listing (2018) is a material governance positive. As a public company, At-Tahur files audited financial statements, is subject to PSX disclosure requirements, and has a visible equity valuation. This transparency materially reduces information asymmetry versus the other two (unlisted) issuers in this comparison.

Pasteurized vs. UHT — The Strategic Bet

Pasteurized milk commands a premium but requires refrigeration and has a shorter shelf life (~7 days). UHT (ultra-high temperature) has a 3-month shelf life but destroys nutritional proteins. PREMA has bet on the pasteurized segment — currently ~3% of processed milk — betting on a consumer health-consciousness shift. This is a high-margin, low-volume strategy that requires patient capital and a cold chain. If the bet is right, the company captures premium pricing power. If wrong, it remains a niche operator.

Sales Tax Headwind (Critical): The government imposed an 18% sales tax on packaged milk in FY25. The IM explicitly acknowledges this hurt revenue growth: "The imposition of 18% sales tax on milk products adversely impacted the Company's sales growth, as consumers shifted to cheaper, untaxed loose milk." FY25 revenue was PKR 10,651 Mn — essentially flat versus FY24's PKR 10,562 Mn despite volume expansion. The company projects recovery via herd expansion and policy normalization. This is a regulatory risk that is sector-wide but asymmetrically hits premium packaged players more than commodity loose milk.

Projections appear optimistic: FY26E projects revenue of PKR 13,843 Mn (+30% YoY) and PAT of PKR 1,696 Mn (+221% YoY). This is a very aggressive ramp. Q1 FY26 data (PKR 2,740 Mn revenue = 19.8% run-rate ahead of FY25) suggests early momentum, but the full-year projection demands sustained 30% growth — above recent trend.

The Risk-Return Map

Ten Key Lessons from These Three Transactions

On Credit Spreads
  1. Same rating ≠ same risk. DPEBSL and Euro Oil both carry A-/A2 entity ratings but have fundamentally different credit profiles. The rating alone is insufficient — structure, security, and sector context must all be analyzed.
  2. Spread = Structure + Credit + Liquidity. Never decompose a spread into only the credit risk component. DPEBSL's 250 bps is primarily a structural (unsecured) premium, not a credit quality premium.
  3. Initial ratings command novelty premiums. Euro Oil at 150 bps for a first-time issuer is arguably mispriced. The market should demand more for the absence of rating history.
  4. Amortizing structures should price tighter. At-Tahur's quarterly amortization reduces investor exposure over time — this is a genuine risk reduction that justifies the tighter 130 bps spread.
  5. Rate environment context is non-negotiable. A 250 bps spread at 13.55% all-in means different things than 250 bps at 24.5% (peak rate). Always frame spread analysis in the absolute yield context.
On Credit Risk Assessment
  1. Short-term instruments require liquidity analysis, not just solvency. A fundamentally sound company can fail to repay a 6-month bullet due to timing mismatches. Current ratio and NWC are the primary metrics — not D/E.
  2. Receivables quality demands scrutiny. DPEBSL's 143% receivables spike is the most analytically important data point in these three IMs. Context (government O&M receivables) mitigates but does not eliminate the concern.
  3. Collateral quality varies enormously. At-Tahur's biological assets are legally "secured" but practically difficult to enforce at full value. DPA accounts (Euro Oil) are more immediately enforceable than physical assets.
  4. Revenue forecasts in new segments require a haircut. DPEBSL's waste management revenue and At-Tahur's FY26E projections both embed significant growth from new/recent activities. Analysts should haircut forecasted revenues from nascent segments by 30–50% in stress scenarios.
  5. Duration risk is minimal here — credit risk dominates. For all three 6–12 month instruments, modified duration is below 0.75 years. The investor's risk is concentrated entirely in credit default — not interest rate movements. Focus your energy accordingly.
Synthesized Takeaway — For the Fixed Income Analyst
These three live transactions from Pakistan's debt capital market in 2025–2026 illustrate the core tension of credit investing: all three instruments carry the same A1 instrument rating, yet they present materially different risk-return profiles. The analyst who relies only on the rating will be under-compensated for risk in the Euro Oil transaction, adequately compensated in DPEBSL, and likely over-compensated in At-Tahur (which arguably deserves a tighter spread given its pristine balance sheet). The 5C Framework, combined with rigorous ratio analysis and structural assessment, is what distinguishes the informed investor from one who simply reads a rating letter. In Pakistan's evolving sukuk market — growing rapidly from a base of bank-dominated intermediation — these analytical skills are increasingly valuable as the QIB market deepens and pricing becomes more nuanced.
