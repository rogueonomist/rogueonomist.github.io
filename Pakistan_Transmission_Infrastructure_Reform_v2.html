Transmission Infrastructure

The Rs.510 Billion Reform Imperative: Unlocking Pakistan's North–South Corridor

Pakistan's transmission system is the single largest structural inefficiency in its power sector. Congestion on the south-to-north corridor forces reliance on expensive northern generation, leaves 519 GWh of wind energy stranded annually, and keeps a Rs.97.8 billion HVDC asset — the ±660 kV, 886 km Matiari–Lahore line — running at 35% of rated capacity. The NTDC Transmission Investment Plan offers the path out, if executed.

Executive Summary

Three Compounding Failures, One Solvable Problem

Pakistan's power crisis is not primarily a generation crisis — it is a transmission crisis. The country has commissioned ~46,600 MW of installed capacity and built a Rs.97.8 billion-per-year HVDC backbone. Both assets are systematically underutilised because the alternating-current network connecting generation to load is inadequate, overloaded, and — in several critical cases — still incomplete. The Rs.510 billion NTDC Transmission Investment Plan is not a cost burden; it is the mechanism for recovering Rs.70–100 billion in annual system-cost losses already embedded in the existing asset base.

Failure 1
North–South Corridor Congestion. The 500 kV HVAC corridor can carry only 1,800 MW under N-0 conditions vs. 4,500 MW combined AC/DC summer ATC potential. Routine curtailment of up to 1,750 MW of cheap southern generation forces dispatch of costly RLNG and RFO plants in the north — a direct fuel-cost premium passed through to consumers.
Failure 2
HVDC Underutilisation. The ±660 kV, 4,000 MW Matiari–Lahore HVDC line — costing Rs.97.8 billion in fixed capacity payments in FY2023-24 — ran at an average of just 34.93% utilisation in FY2024-25. Peak month: 55.12% (July). Trough: 19.99% (November). The Lahore North 500 kV grid station — required since the HVDC's September 2021 commissioning — was still not fully operational three years later.
Failure 3
Wind Evacuation Failure. 519 GWh of zero-marginal-cost wind energy was physically curtailed in FY2024-25, generating Rs.12.8 billion in Non-Project Missed Volume (NPMV) payments. Every rupee of NPMV is 100% transmission-driven: all 36 wind IPPs share a single 220 kV evacuation point at Jhimpir Grid Station, rated at 900 MW against a fleet peak dispatch potential of 1,400–1,600 MW.
The Core Mechanism

Why Transmission Failure Inflates Every Electricity Bill

Pakistan's economic dispatch merit order places nuclear and coal at the bottom (lowest marginal cost), followed by hydro, then gas, then RLNG, with RFO at the top. All least-cost generation — nuclear at KANUPP/K2/K3 and coal at Port Qasim, CPHGC, and Hub — is concentrated in the South. Peak demand sits in Punjab and KPK, 800–1,200 km north.

When the 500 kV AC corridor can only carry 1,800 MW, the system must dispatch roughly 1,750 MW of expensive northern RLNG and gas plants to cover the gap. The marginal cost difference between southern coal (Rs.10–14/kWh all-in) and northern RLNG (Rs.18–22/kWh) multiplied by 1,750 MW running for several thousand hours annually generates Rs.35–70 billion in excess generation cost per year — paid by consumers through the quarterly fuel charge adjustment.

The single most important investment Pakistan can make in electricity affordability is not a new power plant. It is completing the 500 kV Lahore North Grid Station and upgrading the 500 kV AC corridor to unlock the HVDC capacity that has been fully commissioned — and fully paid for — since 2021.

The Central Problem

The North–South Corridor: A Rs.35–70 Billion Annual Dispatch Premium

The cheapest electricity Pakistan can produce — coal from Port Qasim and Hub, nuclear from KANUPP and K2/K3 — is stranded in the South. The most expensive electricity — RLNG at Rs.18–22/kWh marginal cost — runs continuously in the North to fill the gap. The mechanism for bridging this cost gap, the ±660 kV Matiari–Lahore HVDC line, is operating at one-third of rated capacity because the AC network supporting it is inadequate.

HVDC–AC Interaction & N-1 Safety Constraint

The ±660 kV HVDC is a bipolar Line-Commutated Converter (LCC) system — each pole carries 2,000 MW. Under the N-1 contingency (one pole trips), the surviving pole can carry 2,200 MW overload. The AC corridor must immediately absorb the remaining 1,800 MW (= 4,000 − 2,200 MW). This is the irreducible AC requirement for safe full-HVDC operation.

Engineering note: The 4,500 MW total ATC is a stability-constrained system limit, not a simple arithmetic sum. It holds across operating points (rows 1–3). At HVDC = 4,000 MW and AC = 500 MW, the N-1 requirement (1,800 MW AC) is severely violated — a single pole trip would cause ~1,500 MW uncompensated imbalance, risking transient instability and cascade. This is precisely why the NTDC report mandates an AC corridor enhancement of ≥ 1,800 MW before HVDC can safely operate at full rated power.

Cost Quantification

The Annual Price of Congestion: Rs.55–100 Billion

Three distinct cost channels flow from North–South corridor congestion. They are mutually exclusive and non-overlapping:

1. Dispatch substitution premium
~1,750 MW × 4,000–5,000 hrs/yr × Rs.5–8/kWh marginal cost differential (RLNG vs. coal/nuclear) Rs.35–70 Bn/yr
2. Wind NPMV (evacuation failure)
504 GWh × weighted avg NPMV rate Rs.25.4/kWh; separate from dispatch substitution above Rs.12.8 Bn/yr
3. T&T losses — total system cost
3.38 TWh lost × blended variable generation cost Rs.5–7/kWh (hydro-heavy fuel mix) Rs.17–25 Bn/yr
Total annual system cost from congestion Rs.65–108 Bn/yr
On T&T losses: 3.38 TWh of losses at 2.52% average (FY2023-24). The annual cost of Rs.17–25 billion uses a blended variable generation cost of Rs.5–7/kWh, reflecting Pakistan's hydro-heavy generation mix in peak months. At full RLNG marginal cost (Rs.18–22/kWh), the cost rises to Rs.60–75 billion — but losses do not all occur at peak RLNG hours. The incremental saving from improving T&T from 2.52% to 2.0% (a 696 GWh/yr reduction) is Rs.3.5–5 billion annually at the same blended rate.

Note on HVDC sunk costs: The PMLTC capacity payment of Rs.97.8 billion per year is contractually fixed (take-or-pay) regardless of HVDC utilisation. Improving utilisation from 35% to 60% does not reduce this payment. The consumer benefit of higher HVDC utilisation is captured entirely in the dispatch substitution saving (item 1 above) — cheaper southern electrons replacing costlier northern ones. Listing the Rs.97.8 billion as a separate "saving" would be double-counting.

Renewable Energy Evacuation

Wind Curtailment: Paying Twice for Electricity That Never Reached the Grid

Pakistan's 36-plant, 1,843 MW wind fleet generated 3.84 TWh in FY2024-25 at a fleet CUF of 23.8% — the lowest of any technology class in CPPA-G. Against Rs.132.8 billion in fixed capacity obligations, every percentage point of CUF lost to grid curtailment translates directly to a higher effective capacity cost per unit. The NPMV data is diagnostic: 519 GWh physically curtailed (contractual basis: 504 GWh) — every megawatt-hour attributable to a single 220 kV substation at Jhimpir, not to wind intermittency.

Lowest of all technology classes. Nuclear: 78.5%. Thar coal: 72.9%. WAPDA hydro: 45.6%. Bagasse: 30.1%. Against a Jhimpir-Gharo corridor wind resource of 30–35% theoretical CUF, the 6–11 percentage-point deficit is entirely attributable to AC evacuation constraints.

The Bottleneck

Jhimpir GIS: 900 MW Rated, 1,600 MW at Peak

All 36 wind IPPs operate within a 150 km coastal corridor between Jhimpir and Gharo in Sindh. The primary interconnection is Jhimpir Grid Station (220 kV), rated at approximately 900 MW — while the fleet's combined peak dispatch potential reaches 1,400–1,600 MW during Q1 and Q4 peak wind seasons. The Matiari 500 kV hub (the HVDC anchor) is not the constraint — the HVDC is rated at 4,000 MW while peak wind dispatch is only ~900 MW. The constraint is the 220 kV AC sub-transmission between wind farms and Matiari.

A single transformer trip or busbar protection operation at Jhimpir GIS simultaneously curtails 600–900 MW of wind generation. ISMO currently allocates curtailment without an explicit cost-minimisation protocol — curtailing high-tariff Upfront plants (avg NPMV Rs.34.68/kWh) at the same rate as low-tariff Cost-Plus plants (avg NPMV Rs.18.22/kWh). The 2.5× tariff gap makes sequencing the most valuable operational lever.

Zero-capex fix available now: Reallocating 194,780 MWh of curtailment from Upfront plants (avg Rs.39.46/kWh NPMV) to Cost-Plus plants (avg Rs.19.84/kWh NPMV) saves Rs.3.8 billion annually — no infrastructure, no renegotiation, a NEPRA operational directive to ISMO is sufficient.

Flagship Project

500 kV Lahore North Grid Station: Four Years Late, Still the Most Critical Project in Pakistan

The 500 kV Lahore North Grid Station — ADB-financed — was contractually required to be operational before or simultaneously with the HVDC line's September 2021 commissioning. It was not. As of June 2025 — 46 months later — the project stood at 96% physical completion.

Without Lahore North, the existing Lahore AC network cannot safely absorb more than 2,700 MW from the HVDC converter station (at Sundar, Lahore). Running the HVDC above this level reduces the AC corridor below the 1,800 MW N-1 safety floor — making the system vulnerable to a single pole trip causing a cascading instability event. ISMO is therefore forced to constrain HVDC flows, leaving cheap southern generation stranded and expensive northern RLNG running.

Estimated cost of the four-year delay: If the Lahore North GS had been operational from October 2021 and the HVDC had run at 55% utilisation instead of 35%, approximately 5,000 additional GWh per year of cheap southern energy would have reached Punjab. At a marginal displacement cost of Rs.8/kWh (RLNG avoided), this represents approximately Rs.160 billion in accumulated consumer cost over four years that was never saved. This is the true cost of project execution failure.

Economic Analysis

The Transmission Investment Payback: Rs.510 Billion In, Rs.70 Billion Per Year Out

The NTDC TIP is persistently framed as a cost burden. The correct frame is the opposite: it is the mechanism through which Pakistan recovers annual system-cost losses embedded in dispatch inefficiency, wind NPMV, and transmission losses. Three mutually exclusive and non-overlapping benefit streams are quantified below. The HVDC sunk cost (Rs.97.8 Bn/yr fixed) is not a separate benefit — it is the same cost that motivates the dispatch substitution saving.

Rs.35–70 Bn/yr
Dispatch substitution saving. Full N-S corridor unlocks ~1,750 MW of additional cheap southern generation (coal, nuclear), displacing RLNG/RFO in the north. Marginal cost differential Rs.5–8/kWh × 4,000–5,000 operating hours.
Rs.14–18 Bn/yr
Wind NPMV elimination. Full Jhimpir evacuation infrastructure (GIS expansion + 2nd corridor) eliminates 519 GWh curtailment and Rs.12.8 Bn NPMV. Additional 600–900 GWh of zero-marginal-cost generation benefits the system.
Rs.3.5–5 Bn/yr
T&T loss reduction (incremental). Infrastructure improvement targets 2.52% → 2.0% losses (696 GWh/yr reduction). At blended variable cost Rs.5–7/kWh, annual incremental saving = Rs.3.5–5 Bn. Separate from the Rs.17–25 Bn total current cost of all T&T losses.

Reform Sequencing

Three-Phase Reform Roadmap: Infrastructure → Operations → Market

Transmission reform cannot be delivered in a single step. The sequence matters: infrastructure without operational optimisation wastes assets; market design without adequate infrastructure has nothing to price. The three-phase roadmap below is ordered to deliver the fastest benefit with the most defensible engineering logic.

Phase 2 — 2026–2027: Operational Optimisation
Q1–Q2 2026
STATCOM / SVC at Matiari and Lahore Converter Stations
LCC HVDC converters consume reactive power equal to 50–60% of their active power — a fixed physics of thyristor-based Line-Commutated Converters. At 2,700 MW HVDC loading, the reactive demand on the Matiari and Lahore AC buses is 1,350–1,620 MVAr. STATCOMs provide dynamic reactive compensation, reduce AC bus voltage fluctuations, and allow HVDC loading to be pushed higher without voltage instability. Capex: USD 30–60 million.
Q2–Q4 2026
Reactive Power Market — Phase 1 (NEPRA Act §23H(c))
Establishes Pakistan's first ancillary services market with reactive power as the inaugural product. Creates a price signal for voltage support, incentivises generator investment in compensation equipment, and embeds market discipline into ISMO transition. Estimated annual market cost: Rs.8–12 Bn. Estimated benefit: Rs.15–30 Bn.
Q3 2026 – Q2 2027
Moro–RYK & Sheikh Muhammadi–Tarbela Second Lines
Removes the remaining 500 kV chokepoints on the north-south corridor. Enables full N-1-safe dispatch of southern generation with Guddu plants on full load. Currently in PC-I preparation — land acquisition and contracting must begin immediately to hit this window.
Q4 2026 – Q2 2027
Frequency Reserves & Black Start Market — Phase 2
Extends ancillary services to frequency containment and black start restoration services, aligned with ISMO's transition to full market operations. Provides the institutional framework for BESS integration as grid-scale storage comes online.
