Executive Summary
The Structural Problem and Its Solution

Pakistan has a structural petroleum crisis. Oil price shocks cascade through households, inflation, and foreign exchange reserves. On March 13, 2026, Dubai crude doubled from $71 to $146/barrel in 14 days. On April 2, crude spiked again by Rs134 as geopolitical tensions blocked the Strait of Hormuz, stopping 20% of global oil shipments.

For Ahmed, a delivery rider earning Rs35,000/month, the difference between Rs2,000 and Rs3,500 weekly fuel costs is the margin between paying rent or school fees. 30 million motorcycles absorb these shocks first, hardest, and longest.

The solution exists and is already cheaper: Electric two-wheelers, buses, cars, solar pumps, and battery storage have fallen 30–95% in cost over the past decade. An electric motorcycle ($400–600) now costs the same as a petrol CD-70 ($500–700), but charging costs Rs15–24 for 60–80 km while petrol costs Rs70–80. Over a year, Ahmed saves Rs25,000–40,000.

The cycle is predictable and reactive. In 2008, crude hit $140/barrel. In 2022, after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, it touched $120. Each time, Pakistan absorbs the shock, passes some through, subsidises the rest, borrows more, destroys purchasing power through inflation, and waits for prices to fall.

The Economics Have Turned
Technology Cost Falls of 30–95% Over Past Decade

The cost curve inflection point has passed. Electric end-use technologies—motorcycles, cars, buses, solar pumps, batteries—have fallen in cost by 30–95% over the past decade. This is not a projection. It has already happened. The operating cost advantage now favours electric in most applications. Adoption is determined by policy and financing, not economics.

Note on Bus Economics: The 80 MT/year displacement factor and TCO calculations above assume high-utilization urban BRT systems operating at ~110,000 km/year (e.g., Bogotá TransMilenio model). The Capacity Surplus
Pakistan's Grid: Idle Capacity Paying Capacity Charges While Importing Diesel

A natural question follows: if we electrify millions of vehicles, where does the electricity come from?

The answer is embarrassing in its simplicity: the capacity is already there. Whether by design or sheer accident, Pakistan has massive surplus power capacity at precisely the moment the world is scrambling for more.

The playbook exists. The tools are proven. The question is political will, not feasibility.

Indonesia exempted electric vehicle imports from duties. Bangladesh scaled e-rickshaws to 2M+ units. India's FAME-II programme achieved 1M+ annual electric two-wheeler sales in three years by using a petroleum cess — the exact mechanism Pakistan has available through the PDL but which is tragically misallocated to budgetary support rather than the energy transition.

Wartime Mobilisation
The Aggressive Scenario: From 15% to 20% Fuel Displacement

The base-case strategy is deliberately conservative. It assumes gradual adoption curves, institutional friction, and the political inertia that defines Pakistani policymaking. But there exists an aggressive scenario, where the government treats electrification not as aspiration but as strategic necessity.

Two interventions, pushed to their logical limits, change the fiscal arithmetic dramatically.

Political Economy
The technology exists. The financing is available. The demand is there to be tapped. The question is whether the policy machinery will move. Why should the policy machinery care when they do not ride a bike to work like the masses? The indictment is that 30M motorcycle riders, 1M farmers, and 240M citizens have been offered no alternative because the policy machinery has not moved.
A Choice, Not a Forecast
The Decision Ahead

Ahmed, the delivery rider, does not think about trade deficits or electro-tech cost curves. He thinks about the Rs6,000 monthly fuel difference. He thinks about whether to fill the tank, or buy groceries, or pay school fees. He does not know that an electric motorcycle would save him Rs3,000 a month, or that the grid has enough surplus power to charge every bike in Lahore without building a single new power plant. Nobody has told him. Nobody has given him the option.

That is the indictment. The technology does exist. The financing is available. The demand is there. What does not exist is the policy machinery that connects these capabilities to the transition.

Choice Set
Pakistan can continue importing $14B of petroleum annually, absorbing oil shocks reactively, patching fiscal holes with ad-hoc subsidies, watching regional comparators pull ahead.

Or it can enable investment in an electrification strategy that pays for itself within an electoral term, reduces the import bill by 15–20%, converts idle power plants from fiscal liabilities into productive assets, and gives Ahmed an actual choice. This is a structural pivot. The electro-tech economics makes sense. The infrastructure base already exists. We just have to move now and not be bogged down by indecision.

Ahmed will not wait forever. Neither will the balance of payments.
