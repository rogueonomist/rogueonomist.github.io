Section 1
Executive Summary & Product Rationale
NCGCL's guarantee mandate has, to date, been deployed primarily to de-risk Financial Institutions extending credit to MSMEs. The Supplier Credit Guarantee Facility (SCGF) introduces a structurally distinct delivery channel: NCGCL extends its AAA guarantee directly to large anchor corporates — enabling them to extend larger, longer, and more confident trade credit to their SME buyer networks. The guarantee simultaneously enables the anchor to optimize its balance sheet, access better bank financing rates, and reduce IFRS 9 impairment charges. Net effect: credit reaches SMEs faster, more cheaply, and at greater scale than the bank intermediation route.

Why This Product Exists — The Market Failure

Pakistan's MSME credit gap is structural, not cyclical. Banks underserve SMEs due to collateral deficits, documentation gaps, and thin credit histories. But many of these same SMEs already transact with large, creditworthy anchor corporates — OMCs, FMCG companies, logistics providers — on informal or minimally structured credit terms. The anchor knows its SME buyers better than any bank ever will: purchase history, payment behavior, order volumes, seasonal patterns.

The constraint is not information. It is risk absorption capacity. Anchors cannot extend more credit because their Boards, credit committees, and auditors limit uncollateralized receivables exposure. Their banks will not advance against unguaranteed SME receivables at reasonable rates. And their P&L absorbs IFRS 9 ECL charges annually on that receivables book.

NCGCL's AAA guarantee resolves all three constraints simultaneously — which no other market participant can do.

  • ✅   IFRS 9 ECL provision sharply reduced on guaranteed portion

Key Lessons from Global Practice
  1. Anchor skin-in-the-game is non-negotiable. Programs that guaranteed 100% of receivables experienced moral hazard — anchors extended credit to uncreditworthy buyers. 20–30% retention by anchor is the global standard.
  2. Anchor's existing buyer data is the underwriting backbone. Mexico's NAFIN model succeeded because anchors vouched for buyer creditworthiness using transaction history — not bank-style credit reports.
  3. Digitization drives scale. KODIT's supply chain programs scaled 10× after digital invoice integration — claim verification was automated, reducing per-claim costs 70%.
  4. Portfolio-level coverage outperforms transaction-level coverage for cost efficiency and underwriting discipline — particularly for high-volume, small-ticket anchor portfolios.
  5. Anchor receivables financing (reverse factoring) is the natural institutional upgrade — once guarantee is in place, bank discounting at lower spread follows organically.
Board Framing
SCGF is NCGCL's entry into the real economy liquidity channel — distinct from, and complementary to, bank-intermediated lending. Where NCGCL's existing products de-risk banks, SCGF de-risks the supply chain itself. The anchor corporate becomes NCGCL's distribution partner: its deep commercial relationships, real-time buyer data, and existing credit infrastructure substitute for the bank origination channel — at a fraction of the cost and at far greater speed.

Product Architecture — Hybrid Tri-Party Structure
SCGF operates as a tri-party arrangement: NCGCL issues a Master Guarantee Facility to the Anchor Supplier; the Anchor deploys structured credit lines to its SME buyers within NCGCL-approved parameters; the Anchor's bank advances against the NCGCL-guaranteed receivables at near-investment-grade pricing. Three distinct value flows — guarantee, credit, and liquidity — operate simultaneously within one structure.

The Three Flows — Explained

Flow A — Guarantee

NCGCL → Anchor

NCGCL issues a Master Guarantee Facility Agreement (MAGFA) to the Anchor. This instrument guarantees a defined percentage (typically 70–80%) of the Anchor's trade receivables from eligible SME buyers, subject to portfolio eligibility criteria, concentration limits, and reporting covenants. The MAGFA defines the total guaranteed portfolio limit, tenor, fee, and claims mechanics.

The NCGCL guarantee is the foundational instrument — it unlocks all downstream benefits for both anchor and buyers.

Flow B — Trade Credit

Anchor → SME Buyer

The Anchor extends structured credit to its existing SME customers. Credit limits, tenor, and terms are set by the Anchor within NCGCL-approved guardrails (minimum retention, concentration limits, buyer eligibility). NCGCL does not underwrite individual buyers — the Anchor retains this function, using its commercial relationship, transaction history, and internal credit scoring as the basis.

The Anchor retains 20–30% uncovered exposure per buyer, preserving underwriting discipline.

Flow C — Bank Liquidity

Anchor's Bank → Anchor

The Anchor presents its NCGCL-guaranteed receivables to its bank for discounting or reverse factoring. Because the receivables now carry an effective AAA guarantee on 70–80% of face value, the bank prices them at near-investment-grade spread — 150–250 bps lower than unguaranteed SME receivables. This unlocks significant working capital for the Anchor, funding the expanded credit it extends to buyers.

SBP's recognition of NCGCL guarantees as liquid assets reinforces this channel.

Risk Layering Architecture

SCGF is structured with explicit skin-in-the-game at the anchor level. The risk distribution is as follows:

Party Risk Layer Coverage Rationale
Anchor Supplier First-loss (uncovered retention) 20–30% of each receivable / portfolio Preserves origination discipline; anchor bears first losses on its own underwriting decisions
NCGCL Senior guarantee layer 70–80% of portfolio (subject to portfolio cap) AAA guarantee de-risks the majority of the book; enables bank discounting and IFRS 9 ECL reduction
Anchor's Bank Working capital provider Advances against guaranteed receivables Bank bears liquidity risk only (not credit risk on guaranteed portion); prices accordingly
Why 70–80% Coverage?
Global precedent (NAFIN, KODIT, IFC supply chain programs) converges on 70–80% as the optimal coverage ratio: sufficient to materially reduce ECL provisioning and enable bank financing, while retaining enough anchor exposure (20–30%) that credit quality is self-enforced. At 100% coverage, underwriting discipline collapses — anchors extend credit to borderline buyers without consequence.

SCGF's master architecture can be adapted to sector-specific commercial realities. The guarantee mechanics are identical — what differs is buyer profile, credit tenor, invoice structure, fee model, and concentration rules. Below are detailed sub-product designs for priority sectors, followed by cross-sector applicability notes.

SCGF-OMC — Oil Marketing Company Dealer Credit Guarantee

Sub-Product 1 · Pilot Recommended

Context: Pakistan's OMC sector (PSO, Total, Attock, Hascol, PUMA) operates through a network of ~12,000 petrol stations, the large majority of which are SME dealers. Dealers purchase petroleum products from OMCs on 30–45 day credit against a letter of comfort or personal guarantee. OMC exposure to dealer receivables is enormous — PSO alone carries PKR 80–120Bn in trade receivables at any given time.

The Constraint: OMC Boards cap dealer credit lines conservatively. Dealers with strong payment histories cannot grow their business because their credit limit is tied to the OMC's risk appetite, not their own commercial potential. Informal fuel financing (through third-party dealers lending to smaller stations) carries 25–35% effective cost.

Key Sector Data (Indicative)

Average dealer credit line: PKR 15–40M. Credit tenor: 30–45 days. OMC receivables delinquency (90+DPD): 3–6% of book. IFRS 9 ECL on dealer receivables: 2.5–5% of outstanding, depending on economic cycle. Dealer count per major OMC: 1,500–3,000 active dealers.

Fee Model: Model A (Anchor Pays)
OMC pays NCGCL guarantee fee. OMC benefits from: (1) larger dealer volumes enabled by higher credit limits; (2) IFRS 9 ECL reduction on guaranteed book; (3) better bank financing rate on guaranteed receivables. Net economics are strongly positive for OMC — see Section 5.

SCGF-LGST — Logistics Company Deferred Service Credit Guarantee

Sub-Product 2 · Pilot Recommended

Context: Pakistan's logistics sector (TCS, Leopard, Trax, M&P, industrial 3PLs) operates on tight working capital. Anchor logistics companies service e-commerce platforms, FMCG distributors, industrial shippers — most of whom are SMEs. The logistics company provides services today and invoices on 30–60 day terms. Collection risk from SME clients is the primary P&L drag.

The Constraint: Logistics companies restrict SME credit lines aggressively because they bear 100% of collection risk. Many SME shippers are forced to prepay, locking up their working capital. NCGCL guarantee enables the logistics anchor to extend deferred payment terms to SME clients, unlocking SME cash flow for business growth.

Fee Model: Model B (Buyer Pays)
SME buyer (shipper) pays a credit access fee embedded in service pricing. Logistics anchor with tight margins cannot absorb guarantee fee — but SME values deferred payment terms over prepayment requirement. Effective SME cost: 12–16% p.a. vs. 30–40% for informal alternatives.
Unique Structural Advantage
Logistics companies have real-time proof of delivery (POD) data — the cleanest invoice verification in any sector. Claims documentation is digital and auditable. This reduces NCGCL's moral hazard exposure significantly.

SCGF-FMCG — Distributor Trade Credit Guarantee

Sub-Product 3 · Pilot Recommended

Context: Pakistan's FMCG sector (Unilever, Nestlé, P&G, Reckitt, Engro Foods, National Foods) distributes through a multi-tier network of regional distributors and sub-distributors — the vast majority of whom are SMEs. FMCGs extend 30–60 day credit to distributors, who in turn extend shorter credit to retailers. FMCG trade receivables are among the most predictable in Pakistan — distributor churn is low and transaction histories are long.

The Opportunity: FMCG distributors are credit-hungry and deeply embedded in supply chains. Longer credit terms directly translate to more inventory purchases and deeper market penetration. FMCG anchors benefit from volume growth and reduced distributor churn — both of which are significant commercial drivers.

Fee Model: Model C (Split)
FMCG anchor pays 0.75% p.a. base guarantee fee; distributor pays 0.50% credit access premium embedded in extended-term pricing. Both parties share the benefit symmetrically. Net cost to FMCG: offset by ECL reduction and volume uplift.
Added Structural Value

FMCG anchors have rich distributor data — order history, sell-through rates, territory performance. This data can be shared with NCGCL (with consent) to build a sector-level credit scoring model, eventually enabling faster MAGFA onboarding for new FMCG anchors.

Section 6
IFRS 9 — ECL Impairment Benefit for Anchor Corporates
One of the most commercially compelling aspects of SCGF — and perhaps the most underappreciated — is its impact on an anchor corporate's IFRS 9 provisioning obligations. This section provides a Board-level overview of the mechanism, the scale of benefit, and why it makes the NCGCL guarantee economically rational for anchors even before volume and banking benefits are factored in.

IFRS 9 — How ECL Works on Trade Receivables

Under IFRS 9 (effective in Pakistan for all listed companies and most large corporates), entities must recognize Expected Credit Losses (ECL) on financial assets — including trade receivables — from the moment they arise. Unlike IAS 39 which required a loss event to trigger provisioning, IFRS 9 is forward-looking: companies must estimate and provision for losses they expect to occur, even before they do.

For trade receivables, IFRS 9 prescribes a simplified approach using a provision matrix — mapping the probability of default to days past due buckets. The result is that a large corporate holding billions in trade receivables from SME buyers must recognize a significant annual ECL charge on its P&L, even if actual defaults are low.

Stage 1 — Performing

Receivables current or ≤30 DPD. ECL = 12-month expected loss. For SME trade receivables at 2–4% PDO, this is 0.5–2% provision on outstanding.

Stage 2 — Significant Credit Risk Increase

31–90 DPD, or qualitative trigger. ECL = Lifetime expected loss. Provision jumps to 5–15% — a significant P&L charge for anchors with large SME books.

Stage 3 — Credit-Impaired

90+ DPD or objective evidence of impairment. ECL = Full lifetime expected loss net of recovery. Typically 40–70% of outstanding — near write-off economics.

How NCGCL Guarantee Reduces ECL — The Mechanism

IFRS 9 requires ECL to be measured net of credit enhancements that mitigate loss — explicitly including third-party guarantees. When NCGCL guarantees 75% of a receivable, the anchor's maximum loss on that receivable is capped at 25% (its uncovered retention). The ECL provision for the guaranteed portion is effectively computed against NCGCL's creditworthiness — AAA rated — rather than against the SME buyer's creditworthiness.

Auditor & CFO Considerations

For the IFRS 9 ECL reduction to be recognized in financial statements, anchors must ensure the following:

Requirements for ECL Recognition
  • NCGCL guarantee must be legally enforceable, unconditional (subject only to claims documentation), and transferable.
  • The MAGFA must be designated as a credit enhancement under IFRS 9 — legal opinion required (one-time, per MAGFA template).
  • ECL must be computed net of guarantee for the covered tranche — per IFRS 9 B5.5.54 (credit risk mitigation).
  • NCGCL's AAA rating must be referenced as the counterparty credit quality for the guaranteed portion — standard audit expectation.
  • Disclosure in annual report notes: nature, extent, and terms of credit enhancement. Audit committee briefing recommended before first year-end application.
NCGCL's Role in Supporting Anchors
  • NCGCL will provide a standard IFRS 9 Credit Enhancement Certificate with each MAGFA — confirming coverage ratio, enforceability, and NCGCL's own credit quality.
  • NCGCL will engage a Big-4 auditor for a one-time opinion on MAGFA structure's compliance with IFRS 9 credit enhancement criteria — shareable with all anchor auditors.
  • NCGCL will provide quarterly portfolio data to support anchor's provision matrix calculations.
  • This advisory support is a material value-add for anchor CFOs navigating IFRS 9 under audit pressure — it differentiates NCGCL from commercial credit insurers.
Critical Nuance — Anchor Must Not Over-Rely on ECL Benefit
The ECL saving is real and material — but the guarantee should not be used primarily as an accounting device. NCGCL's primary mandate is credit access expansion. Anchors must genuinely extend larger/longer credit to SME buyers as a result of the guarantee. NCGCL will monitor whether portfolio growth (buyer additions, credit limit increases) accompanies the ECL benefit. If an anchor purchases a guarantee solely to reduce provisions without extending incremental credit, NCGCL will not renew the MAGFA.
Section 7
Operational Flow & Implementation
SCGF is designed for operational simplicity. The anchor does the heavy lifting — buyer origination, credit limit setting, invoice management, and first-recovery. NCGCL's operational role is governance, portfolio monitoring, and claims adjudication.

End-to-End Operational Flow

  • 1
    Anchor Onboarding & MAGFA Execution
    Anchor submits application: audited accounts (3 years), existing buyer credit portfolio data, buyer master list, internal credit policy. NCGCL conducts due diligence (7–10 working days). MAGFA drafted, risk reviewed by NCGCL Risk Committee, CEO/Board approval as required. MAGFA executed. Anchor pays first-quarter guarantee fee. Timeline: 30–45 days from application.
  • 2
    Buyer Eligibility Certification
    Anchor submits initial buyer list against NCGCL eligibility criteria. NCGCL reviews for exclusions (negative list, concentration limits). Approved buyer list established — this becomes the eligible universe for the portfolio. Updates monthly via digital portal. NCGCL does not underwrite individual buyers; Anchor certifies eligibility.
  • 3
    Credit Extension to SME Buyers
    Anchor extends credit to eligible buyers per its commercial process. No NCGCL pre-approval required per transaction. Anchor issues invoices; goods/services delivered. Invoice enters Anchor's receivables system. SCGF guarantee automatically covers eligible invoices within portfolio cap and concentration limits. Real-time portfolio register maintained by Anchor; shared with NCGCL monthly.
  • 4
    Bank Receivables Discounting (Flow C)
    Anchor presents guaranteed receivables to its bank for discounting. Bank verifies NCGCL MAGFA validity and coverage. Bank advances against guaranteed receivables at near-investment-grade spread (KIBOR + 150–200 bps vs. KIBOR + 350–450 bps unguaranteed). Anchor liquidity recycled to extend more buyer credit. NCGCL not party to bank agreement; guarantee covers receivable itself, not the bank facility.
  • 5
    Collections & Performing Portfolio Management
    Anchor collects from buyers per normal commercial process. Monthly portfolio report submitted to NCGCL: aging by buyer, DPD buckets, collections, new additions. NCGCL runs quarterly portfolio review. If concentration limits are breached, Anchor must cure within 30 days or NCGCL suspends new additions.
  • 6
    Default Management & First Recovery
    Buyer misses payment → enters Anchor's collections process. Anchor must pursue first recovery: reminder notices, personal visits, payment plans. Anchor documents all attempts. During 60-day waiting period, NCGCL is notified of any buyers entering 60 DPD. Anchor cannot claim before exhausting documented first-recovery process.
  • 7
    Claims Submission & NCGCL Payment
    Buyer at 90 DPD (or insolvency): Anchor submits full claims pack to NCGCL. NCGCL reviews within 15 business days. If complete and compliant: NCGCL pays (Coverage % × outstanding invoice principal) within 30 business days. NCGCL becomes subrogated creditor for the paid amount. Anchor recovers any residual (uncovered retention) independently.
  • 8
    Post-Claim Recovery Sharing
    NCGCL and Anchor jointly pursue defaulted buyer (or write off as appropriate). Any recoveries shared pro-rata (NCGCL: covered %, Anchor: uncovered %). NCGCL recovery efforts through legal notice, SECP/court action if material. Recovery data fed back into portfolio performance analytics and pricing calibration.

Section 8
Risk Register & Mitigants
SCGF introduces a set of risks distinct from NCGCL's bank-intermediated products — primarily because the anchor corporate, not a regulated FI, is the origination and claims-management party. The following register identifies material risks, rates their severity, and specifies structural mitigants.
Risk Severity Description Mitigant
Moral Hazard — Anchor HIGH Anchor extends credit to uncreditworthy buyers, knowing NCGCL absorbs 75% of loss. Classic moral hazard from over-insurance. Mandatory 20–30% anchor retention; performance rebate incentive; portfolio-level loss triggers; real-time delinquency monitoring; annual portfolio audit.
Fraud / Collusion HIGH Anchor fabricates invoices or colludes with buyers to manufacture claims. Most severe tail risk in receivables guarantee programs globally. Physical/digital delivery evidence required per claim; spot audits of 5% of portfolio invoices annually; NCGCL right to buyer-level data; fraud voids guarantee; anchor liable for repayment; criminal referral provisions in MAGFA.
Anchor Credit Deterioration MEDIUM Anchor's own financial health deteriorates — reducing likelihood it will maintain portfolio quality or honor reporting obligations. Anchor financial health covenants in MAGFA; automatic review if anchor credit rating drops below BBB; NCGCL can suspend new additions; guarantee continues for existing receivables only.
Concentration Risk MEDIUM Portfolio becomes concentrated in a single buyer, geography, or sector — increasing correlated default risk. Hard limits per buyer (5%) and sector (30%); NCGCL portfolio analytics monitor concentration continuously; excess above limits not covered.
Systemic / Macro Shock MEDIUM Economic downturn causes correlated SME defaults across multiple anchor portfolios — overwhelming NCGCL capacity. Portfolio caps per anchor; NCGCL total SCGF exposure limit (Board-set, as % of capital); stress testing annually; reserve fund sizing based on correlated stress scenario.
Documentation Risk MEDIUM Anchor submits incomplete or deficient claims documentation — NCGCL unable to pay valid claims promptly, creating reputational damage. Standard claims pack template provided at onboarding; anchor training; digital claims portal with automated completeness checks; 15-day pre-review window before formal submission.
IFRS 9 Recognition Risk LOW Anchor's auditor declines to recognize NCGCL guarantee as IFRS 9 credit enhancement, negating ECL benefit — reducing anchor's commercial incentive. NCGCL to obtain Big-4 legal opinion on MAGFA structure; IFRS 9 Certificate issued with each MAGFA; anchor CFO/auditor briefing at onboarding.
Regulatory / SBP Risk LOW SBP issues new guidance affecting NCGCL's ability to provide guarantees to non-FI entities, or requiring additional capital against SCGF exposure. Pre-consultation with SBP at product design stage; SCGF explicitly framed as MSME credit access product (consistent with SBP's own MSME policy priorities); NCGCL's SBP regulated status is protective.
Reputational Risk LOW High-profile anchor default or claims dispute creates negative market perception of SCGF product. Conservative pilot anchors selected (rated, listed, SBP-regulated or SECP-governed); Board-level disclosure of SCGF terms; proactive media/market communication strategy.
Moral Hazard — The Central Risk, Managed by Design
The 20–30% anchor retention is SCGF's primary risk control — and it is non-negotiable. All global precedents (NAFIN, KODIT, IFC) that reduced retention below 15% experienced portfolio quality deterioration within 18–24 months. NCGCL must hold this line even under commercial pressure from large anchors requesting higher coverage. The performance rebate mechanism (fee reduction for low loss portfolios) is the constructive alternative to higher coverage — it rewards quality origination rather than relaxing risk controls.
