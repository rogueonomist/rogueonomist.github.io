Context
International Fertilizer Markets, 2014–2026
International prices for urea and DAP, sourced from the World Bank Pink Sheet, represent the primary external shock vector for Pakistan's agricultural input costs. Both fertilizers are traded in tight global markets sensitive to energy prices, geopolitical disruptions, and trade policy—particularly Chinese export quotas and Russian supply.

Transmission Mechanism
How International Prices Reach Pakistani Farmers
Pakistan operates two distinct fertilizer regimes with fundamentally different transmission dynamics. Urea is produced domestically under a subsidized gas pricing regime, creating a near-permanent buffer between international and domestic prices. DAP is largely imported and follows international prices more closely — albeit with a structural premium reflecting import costs, dealer margins, and taxes.

Key Finding: Two Completely Different Transmission Regimes
Urea (Subsidized Domestic Production): Local urea in USD terms has been remarkably stable at $250–340/mt since 2018, while international prices ranged from $200 to $925/mt. The subsidized gas allocation to domestic fertilizer plants (FFBL, FFC, Engro) acts as a near-complete firewall. Pre-crisis pass-through coefficient β = 0.033 (statistically zero). Post-crisis β = −0.717 (negative — as international prices spiked, local USD prices actually fell via PKR depreciation). This protection is costly for the exchequer, however.

DAP (Imported, Market-Based): Local DAP consistently trades at 40–65% premium over international, reflecting transport, taxes, and margins. Pre-crisis pass-through β = 0.703 (strong). Both series share Granger causality (F=4.69, p=0.001, 4-month lag). When international DAP spiked, so did local — with no subsidy buffer.
When do prices converge? The urea convergence zones (2018–2019) coincided with periods when: (1) international prices were at historic lows ($200–240/mt), (2) PKR was relatively stable (~PKR 140/USD), and (3) domestic plant capacity was adequate to meet demand. The current divergence (March 2026: international $726/mt, local equivalent ~$310/mt) represents the most extreme gap in the dataset — a 57% discount for Pakistani farmers. This reflects the renewed global urea spike, possibly driven by Russian supply disruptions and new demand cycles.
Transmission to Food
Fertilizer Price → Rice & Wheat Price Linkages
The path from fertilizer to food prices operates through two channels: (1) direct cost channel — fertilizer is ~15–25% of total wheat/rice production costs, so higher input prices squeeze margins and eventually raise prices; (2) support price channel — the government sets minimum procurement prices partly based on production costs, creating a lagged but institutionalized fertilizer-to-food price linkage.

Key Finding: DAP is the Dominant Food Price Driver
DAP → Rice is the strongest and most statistically robust link in the chain (Granger F=10.5, p<0.0001, lag=2 months). The pass-through coefficient from local DAP to local rice prices (β=0.173 in log-log) means a 10% rise in DAP prices leads to a 1.7% rise in rice prices within 2 months, and further effects accumulate over 6–9 months. Wheat price dynamics are more complex due to the support price mechanism — the fertilizer signal works through the annual support price announcement rather than through continuous market pricing.
Policy Channel
Wheat Minimum Support Price & Fertilizer Costs
Pakistan's wheat Minimum Support Price (MSP) is set annually (typically announced October–November for the April harvest). The Ministry of National Food Security explicitly incorporates cost-of-production estimates — including fertilizer costs — into the MSP determination. This creates a lagged but systematic channel through which fertilizer price increases translate into food price increases via government policy rather than direct market forces.

Critical observation: The 2022–23 MSP increase of 95% (PKR 1,950 → PKR 3,800 per 40kg) was the largest in Pakistan's history. It was explicitly justified by input cost increases — particularly fertilizer, fuel, and labour. This created a secondary food inflation channel: even consumers in urban areas who had no direct exposure to fertilizer price changes faced 95% higher wheat floor prices. Structural break test confirms a significant regime shift in the wheat-urea relationship post-2022 (Chow F=7.64, p=0.001).
The Double Transmission Problem
Pakistan's wheat pricing mechanism creates a perverse amplification: fertilizer price increases feed into both (1) direct production costs raising market prices, and (2) the government's own cost-of-production calculations used to set the MSP. This double channel means that a 30% fertilizer price increase may ultimately produce a 15–20% wheat flour price increase — with a 6–12 month lag as the policy adjustment works through the system.

Scenario Analysis
Counterfactual Scenarios & Policy Stress Tests
Three counterfactual scenarios are examined: (1) No fertilizer price shock — what would food prices have looked like? (2) Full pass-through — what would have happened if Pakistan had not subsidized urea during the crisis? (3) Forward scenario — what is the likely food price impact if the current international urea spike ($726/mt in March 2026) continues?

Scenario A: No 2021–23 Fertilizer Shock

−18%

Fertilizer contribution to rice price increase: Without the DAP shock, rice would have averaged PKR 87/kg vs. actual PKR 103/kg during 2021–23. Counterfactual wheat support price: PKR ~2,600/40kg vs actual PKR 3,800/40kg in 2022–23.

Scenario B: Full Urea Price Pass-Through (No Subsidy)

+45%

Estimated additional food price increase: If international urea prices had fully passed through to domestic prices (pre-crisis β=0.70 applied to urea too), local urea would have reached ~PKR 7,000–8,000/bag at peak crisis. Estimated additional food inflation: 12–18% beyond what was observed.

Scenario C: Current Spike Continues (2026)

+8–12%

Forward food price risk: International urea at $726/mt (Mar 2026) represents a new shock. If local urea prices adjust upward (subsidy constraint tightens), food prices face 8–12% upside risk. PKR stability (~PKR 280/USD) limits the amplification effect seen in 2022.

Policy Implications
Recommendations for Prime Minister's Office
Core Policy Insight
Pakistan currently operates a de facto food price insurance scheme through subsidized gas for urea production. This shield has worked — it prevented an estimated 12–18% additional food inflation during the 2021–23 crisis. However, it is an expensive, poorly targeted, and fiscally fragile mechanism. DAP, which has no equivalent protection, transmitted fully through to food prices. The policy architecture needs to be made more robust, more targeted, and better forward-looking.
01

Establish a Fertilizer Price Stabilization Fund

Create a dedicated fund (capitalized at PKR 50–100 billion) that accumulates savings during periods of low international fertilizer prices and disburses targeted subsidies to small farmers (under 12.5 acres) when international prices exceed a trigger threshold. This replaces the blunt gas-subsidy mechanism with a more fiscally transparent and shock-responsive tool. International precedent: India's DBT-fertilizer scheme, Bangladesh's Subsidy Management Fund.

02

Decouple DAP Import Policy from Spot Price Exposure

Since DAP shows strong international pass-through (β=0.795, 4-month lag), Pakistan should establish forward purchasing contracts and a 90-day strategic reserve of DAP. Given that Granger causality runs from international DAP to domestic prices with a 4-month lag, there is an actionable 3–4 month window for policy response. Consider hedging 30–40% of annual DAP import requirements through commodity futures or long-term bilateral supply agreements with producing countries (Morocco, Saudi Arabia, Russia).

03

Reform the Wheat MSP to Include a Fertilizer Cost Indexation Formula

The 95% MSP increase in 2022–23 created massive uncertainty for the flour milling industry and consumer prices. A transparent, pre-announced formula linking MSP to a basket of input costs (fertilizer: 25% weight, fuel: 30%, labour: 25%, seed: 20%) would reduce policy shock and allow supply chain actors to plan. This mechanism already informally exists — formalizing it with quarterly publication of the underlying cost index would improve market predictability.

04

Implement a Fertilizer Price Early Warning Dashboard

International fertilizer prices Granger-cause domestic food prices with 2–4 month lags. This provides a meaningful prediction window. Establish a monthly Early Warning Bulletin (Ministry of Finance + MNFSR) that tracks: international urea and DAP price trends, PKR/USD trajectory, gas supply to domestic fertilizer plants, and projects expected domestic fertilizer prices and downstream food price implications. Pakistan can act within the lag window before prices hit consumers.

05

Reduce Fiscal Dependence on the Implicit Gas Subsidy by Gradually Removing Industrial Gas Subsidies for Large-Scale Producers

Large fertilizer manufacturers (with market capitalization in the hundreds of billions) benefit disproportionately from subsidized gas pricing. The subsidy should be redirected: price gas at market rates for industry, but return the savings as a direct per-bag subsidy to NICL/Kissan Card registered smallholders. This improves targeting without reducing the total subsidy quantum, and removes the perverse incentive for fertilizer companies to expand capacity to capture more subsidized gas.

06

Diversify Domestic Urea Production Away from Gas Dependency

Pakistan's vulnerability stems from a single feedstock chain: subsidized gas → urea → food prices. A portion of domestic fertilizer capacity should be transitioned to coal gasification or renewable-ammonia pathways. Thar coal, already under exploitation, can serve as a feedstock for urea production at costs significantly below current gas import parity prices. A 20% diversification of urea production away from SSGC/SNGPL gas would materially reduce the fiscal burden of the implicit subsidy.

07

Monitor the Current (2026) Urea Price Surge Actively

International urea has already reached $726/mt in March 2026 — 80% above the 2024 average of $397/mt. If this persists, the fiscal cost of the gas subsidy will intensify dramatically. Cabinet should receive a monthly briefing on: (1) the international urea price trend and its drivers, (2) the implicit subsidy cost at current domestic pricing, (3) food price projections under different subsidy scenarios, and (4) the adequacy of current domestic fertilizer production to meet Rabi and Kharif season demand without gap-filling at international prices.

Literature Support
The findings are consistent with a growing body of empirical literature. Studies of South Asian fertilizer-food price linkages (e.g., Minot and Dewina, 2013; Jayne et al., 2018 on African contexts; Hossain and Verbeke, 2010 on Bangladesh) consistently find: (1) fertilizer-food pass-through is partial and lagged, typically 20–40% within 6 months; (2) exchange rate depreciation amplifies pass-through significantly; (3) government procurement/support prices institutionalize the linkage even when market mechanisms are weak; and (4) DAP, as a fully tradable commodity, shows higher pass-through than nitrogen fertilizers subject to domestic price controls. The Pakistan case is consistent with all four stylized facts but shows unusually large amplification during the 2021–23 shock — likely because PKR depreciation and the fertilizer shock coincided, creating a dual adverse supply shock.
Empirical Bottom Line for Policy: A $100/mt increase in international urea prices, if unchecked by subsidy policy, would lead to approximately: (1) +5–8% increase in local urea prices within 2–4 months; (2) +1.5–2.5% increase in rice prices within 4–6 months; (3) +2–4% upward pressure on the wheat MSP, with a 6–12 month institutional lag. The current international urea spike of ~$330/mt above 2024 average is therefore a material food security risk if the domestic subsidy shield weakens.
