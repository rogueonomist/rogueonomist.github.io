Sector Diagnosis
Pakistan's gas sector is facing a compounding fiscal crisis

Three simultaneous structural failures — tariffs set at a fraction of cost recovery, a US$1.3 billion LPG import bill concentrated in commercial users, and subsidised residential gas directed at water heating — compound annually into an unsustainable quasi-fiscal liability exceeding Rs. 1 trillion in accumulated shortfalls.

Three Structural Failures
Pillar I — Tariff Collapse
Protected households pay Rs. 200/MMBtu against a cost of Rs. 1,757/MMBtu
An effective subsidy of Rs. 1,507/MMBtu — an 8.8× tariff gap — for the lowest slab. Accumulated over 12.1 million domestic consumers, this creates Pakistan's largest undisclosed budgetary liability, growing by Rs. 300–400B per year without structural intervention.
SNGPL's proposed flat rate of Rs. 1,757/MMBtu would be the most significant residential tariff restructuring in Pakistan's gas sector history.
Pillar II — LPG Import Burden
US$1.31 billion import bill — two-thirds of which is addressable commercial demand
The 1.47 million tonnes of C&I LPG is not market preference — it is the accumulated effect of gas network exclusion. Commercial users pay Rs. 7,084/MMBtu for LPG when RLNG is available at Rs. 3,652/MMBtu. Converting 90% of this demand yields US$284M/year in net FX savings without a single rupee of subsidy.
Commercial users are now the single largest LPG-consuming category at 42.8% — overtaking households (33.2%). This fundamentally shifts the policy priority.
Pillar III — Subsidised Non-Essential Use
70% of residential gas goes to water and space heating — not cooking
The majority of the residential gas subsidy does not heat a cooking pot — it fuels gas geysers and space heaters at below-cost prices. Full geyser electrification alone would avoid ~US$1.65B/year in RLNG imports. At the Rs. 1,757/MMBtu tariff threshold, heat pump payback falls to 2.9 years — making switching economically rational.
Tariff reform is the trigger: once gas is priced at cost recovery, household electrification becomes self-executing.
Fiscal Architecture
The tariff gap: what consumers pay vs. what gas costs

LPG Market Analysis
A US$1.31 billion import burden — two-thirds in addressable commercial demand

Pakistan Energy Yearbook 2024-25 reveals a structurally transformed LPG market: commercial users (42.8%) now exceed households (33.2%) as the single largest consuming category. The dominant LPG reform target is therefore not rural cooking — it is businesses and industries paying Rs. 7,084/MMBtu who would rationally shift to piped RLNG at Rs. 3,652/MMBtu.

LPG-to-induction: the stovetop water heating factor
LPG households consume ~3.5× more cooking fuel per capita than piped gas households because 30–40% of LPG use is stovetop water heating — LPG households heat water on the stove, not in a dedicated geyser.
Induction conversion for LPG households therefore eliminates both cooking and a significant water-heating load simultaneously — making it particularly high-impact.

Reform Architecture
A four-pillar, sequenced reform generating US$2.5B+ per year by FY2030

The reform architecture integrates four pillars in deliberate sequence. Tariff rationalisation must precede electrification — switching becomes economically irrational if gas remains priced below cost. C&I LPG conversion runs in parallel and requires no subsidy. The sequence: Tariff Reform → Induction Adoption → Heat Pump Scaling.

Implementation Sequencing
Now — FY2026-27
Tariff Rationalisation
SNGPL flat rate Rs. 1,757; SSGC phased escalation. BISP compensatory transfers activated. GDS sukuk bond issuance. New connection moratorium enforced.
FY2026-28
C&I LPG Conversion
50% C&I LPG converted to RLNG (US$158M FX saving). Priority: restaurants, hotels, bakeries, industrial clusters within network reach.
FY2027-29
HP Geyser Programme
Subsidised HP geyser rollout — 40% capital grant, 4 million households by FY2030. ADB/GCF concessional financing. UFG reduction via SCADA upgrades.
FY2030+
Full Reform Impact
US$2.5B+/year FX saving. Gas quasi-fiscal deficit reduced 60%. Residential share of gas sales below 20%. GDS receivable resolved via sukuk absorption.
