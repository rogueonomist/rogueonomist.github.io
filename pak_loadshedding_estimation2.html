How Solar Saved Pakistan From Load Shedding
June–July 2026 Heatwave + RLNG Blockade Stress Test

A temperature-indexed adequacy model showing how behind-the-meter solar can absorb sunshine-hour deficits, conserve scarce RLNG for the post-sunset bridge, and convert a broad all-day load-shedding crisis into a focused evening and late-night dispatch challenge.
Brief overview

BTM solar changes the crisis geometry: daylight shortage disappears; the problem moves to 18:00–02:00.

Under the RLNG blockade assumption, Pakistan has only three LNG cargoes available and no Qatar baseline flows. Without the recent surge in behind-the-meter solar, the system would face material shortages across both daytime and evening hours during a heatwave. With enough BTM solar to absorb the sunshine-hour deficit, load shedding during 09:00–16:00 falls to zero in the default calculator mode. Scarce RLNG can then be rationed for the post-sunset cooling bridge, where BTM solar is gone, residential cooling demand remains high, and hot-night demand can persist beyond midnight.

The structural decline from FY21-22 peak of 30,231 MW to FY24-25 adjusted peak of ~26,617 MW — despite rising temperatures — reflects approximately 3–4 GW of BTM solar displacement on grid peak demand (Stranded BTM Solar Analysis, Ammar H. Khan, 2025).
Key Structural Insight: Pakistan's summer grid peak demand (incl. KE) has declined from 30,231 MW in June 2022 to approximately 26,617 MW in June 2025 — a reduction of ~3,600 MW — even as temperatures have trended warmer (+0.5–1.4°C annual anomaly per PMD). This counter-intuitive pattern is driven by behind-the-meter solar installations suppressing daytime cooling demand at the grid level. Without BTM growth, the grid peak in June 2025 would have likely approached 28,500–30,000 MW.

Demand Data: ISMO/CPPA-G hourly System Demand Profile file (FY 2024-25, July 2024 – June 2025), 8,760 hourly observations, excluding K-Electric. Daily peak = maximum hourly reading per calendar day. Historical FY 2019-20 through FY 2023-24 peaks from NEPRA State of Industry Report 2024 (Table 56), which includes K-Electric.
Temperature Data: Daily Lahore maximum temperature reconstructed from PMD published monthly mean-maximum climate normals (1961–1990 baseline, Lahore Airport WMO 41640) plus PMD-reported year-specific anomalies for Punjab/Lahore (June 2024: +0.5°C; July 2024: +1.1°C; June 2025: +1.4°C estimated). Specific heatwave days anchored to documented observations (Dawn, 9–12 June 2025: Lahore 44–45°C). Caveat: Daily temperature series is reconstructed, not observed. The framework should be re-validated against PMD station data (WMO 41640) when available.
Temperature Sensitivity (350 MW/°C, June; 200 MW/°C, July): Derived from observed demand range during June 2025. Documented peak demand of 23,817 MW at ~44°C vs shoulder-period weekday demand of ~20,000–21,000 MW at ~40–41°C implies approximately 700–950 MW per °C across the 40–44°C range (ISMO basis, excl. KE). A conservative central estimate of 350 MW/°C is applied to reflect that some within-month demand variation is driven by load shedding and non-temperature factors. July coefficient of 200 MW/°C is lower because monsoon cloud cover, humidity, and precipitation suppress the cooling-demand response to temperature.
Structural Baseline for FY 2026: Starting from FY 2024-25 observed peaks, two adjustments are applied: (1) BTM solar suppression: an estimated −600 MW for June and −800 MW for July (cumulative, reflecting ~3–5 GW of new BTM capacity additions at approximately 15–20% daytime grid displacement per GW); (2) Underlying demand growth: +400–500 MW (estimated from economic activity recovery at ~1–2% annual demand growth rate, partially offset by energy efficiency improvements). Net structural change is approximately −200 MW for June and −300 MW for July versus FY25 baseline.
Weekend Discount (~900 MW): Applied for Friday and Sunday. Reflects reduced commercial, institutional and industrial loads. Residential cooling load does not decline proportionally on hot weekends, so the discount is lower on extreme temperature days. Applied as flat adjustment in this framework; a more precise model would make the discount temperature-dependent.
Monsoon Onset Uncertainty: The July scenarios assume the Pakistani monsoon is active and producing rainfall. If the monsoon is significantly delayed (>15 days late, as can occur in El Niño years), July temperatures can remain in the 40–42°C range and peak demand could approach the upper scenario band. A delayed monsoon is the primary tail risk for July 2026.
Solar Bridge Calculator: The calculator assumes no Qatar baseline flows and treats selected LNG cargoes as the only RLNG available for the planning horizon. One cargo is treated as approximately 700 GWh of power-equivalent energy. Hourly demand shape is taken from the uploaded ISMO peak-day profiles: June 20, 2025 for June stress and July 11, 2024 for July stress. Hourly RLNG dispatch is scaled to the OMO-adjusted requirement of 72.7 GWh/day for June and 78.8 GWh/day for July. In the default mode, new BTM solar is assumed to absorb any 09:00–16:00 sunshine-hour deficit, so the model conserves scarce RLNG for the 18:00–02:00 evening and hot-night bridge. This mode materially reduces required cargoes: over a 45-day horizon, 3 cargoes are close to sufficient on a strict energy basis, while 1 additional cargo is recommended as a buffer; over a 60-day horizon, 1 additional cargo is the minimum and 2 are recommended. Users can turn the BTM assumption OFF to see the all-day RLNG shortfall case.
