Transmission Infrastructure

The Rs.510 Billion Reform Imperative: What to Do First, and Why

Pakistan's North–South corridor has 1,750 MW of congestion. One project — the 500 kV Lahore North Grid Station, at 96% physical completion — resolves 1,300 MW of it, or 74%, by unlocking the ±660 kV HVDC line that has been fully paid for and underused since 2021. The remaining 26% requires a separate set of southern AC upgrades. New northern generation (Chashma C5) and accelerating BTM solar change the economics of some TIP projects, but strengthen rather than weaken the case for completing what is already built. This is the prioritised sequencing.

Investment Prioritisation

What to Do First — A Four-Tier Priority Stack

Not all 99 TIP projects carry equal urgency. Three analytical changes — the Lahore North two-constraint decomposition, the Chashma C5 northern generation addition, and accelerating BTM solar — reshape which projects should move immediately, which should be sequenced next, and which deserve fresh economic scrutiny before capital commitment. The stack below is ordered by return, reversibility, and dependence on completion of prior tiers.

The core finding: 74% of Pakistan's North–South congestion can be resolved by completing one project that is already 96% built. That project — Lahore North 500 kV Grid Station — delivers Rs.33–56 billion in annual dispatch savings by unlocking the ±660 kV HVDC line whose full capacity payment of Rs.97.8 billion per year is already being collected from consumers. Every month of further delay compounds an already-accumulated loss of approximately Rs.160 billion over four years. This is not a planning decision. It is an execution emergency.

Executive Summary

Three Compounding Failures, One Near-Complete Solution

Pakistan's power crisis is not primarily a generation crisis — it is a transmission crisis with a specific anatomy. 1,750 MW of cheap southern generation is curtailed daily because the wires connecting it to northern load are inadequate. 74% of that problem is solvable by completing a single substation that is already 96% built and fully financed. The residual 26% requires a separate, sequenced set of southern AC line upgrades. New northern nuclear capacity (Chashma C5) and accelerating BTM solar change the economics of some parts of the Rs.510 billion TIP — but they strengthen the case for the highest-priority projects, which are the ones that unlock infrastructure already commissioned and paid for.

Failure 1
HVDC Underutilisation. The ±660 kV, 4,000 MW Matiari–Lahore line ran at 34.9% average utilisation in FY2024-25 (peak: 55.1% July; trough: 20.0% November). Rs.97.8 billion in fixed capacity payments flow to PMLTC annually regardless. Root cause: Lahore North GS has been absent for four years.
Failure 2
AC Corridor Overloads. The 500 kV Dadu–Shikarpur, Shikarpur–Guddu, and Moro–RYK lines constrain AC southward flow to 1,800 MW at N-0. Even when the HVDC is unconstrained, these lines block the full 4,500 MW summer ATC from being realised. This is the residual 450 MW — the 26% that Lahore North alone does not fix.
Failure 3
Wind Evacuation Failure. 519 GWh curtailed annually at Rs.12.8 billion NPMV — 100% caused by a single 220 kV grid station at Jhimpir rated at 900 MW against a fleet peak dispatch potential of 1,400–1,600 MW. Structurally separate from the N-S corridor; separate infrastructure fix required.

The Central Problem

The North–South Corridor: Two Independent Constraints, One Combined Effect

The NEPRA PER Transmission FY2024-25 explicitly states the congestion has two distinct causes: HVDC underutilisation (Lahore North) and southern AC line overloads (Dadu–Shikarpur, Shikarpur–Guddu, Moro–RYK). These are geographically separate, mechanically independent, and require different infrastructure fixes on different timelines. Treating them as a single problem leads to misallocated capital.

Constraint A — HVDC Side (Northern) · Lahore North · 1,300 MW
1,300 MW
74% of total curtailment
The ±660 kV HVDC Bipole is rated at 4,000 MW. Its LCC (Line-Commutated Converter) inverter station at Sundar, Lahore requires a strong AC network to absorb high DC power transfers. Under N-1 (one pole trips at 2,000 MW), the surviving pole can carry 2,200 MW maximum overload. The Lahore AC network must instantly absorb the remaining 1,800 MW (= 4,000 − 2,200 MW). Without Lahore North GS, this AC absorption capacity does not exist. ISMO caps HVDC at 2,700 MW — meaning 1,300 MW of potential HVDC throughput is permanently constrained. Fix: Lahore North GS commissioning.
Constraint B — AC Side (Southern) · Line Overloads · ~450 MW
~450 MW
26% of total curtailment
The 500 kV AC lines running parallel to the HVDC corridor on the southern side — Dadu–Shikarpur, Shikarpur–Guddu, and Moro–Rahim Yar Khan — are overloaded at N-0 operating conditions. This forces NGC to curtail Port Qasim and Hub China generation even during normal operations, independently of whatever the HVDC is doing. After Lahore North commissions and the HVDC moves toward 4,000 MW, these AC lines become the binding limit on total system southward transfer. Fix: 500 kV line upgrades (Dadu–Shikarpur, Shikarpur–Guddu, Moro–RYK) — sequenced after Constraint A.
Why Constraint A must be fixed before Constraint B: After Lahore North, HVDC can carry 4,000 MW and the remaining AC bottleneck (450 MW) means total transfer reaches only ~4,050–4,200 MW instead of the theoretical 4,500 MW. That remaining gap is worth ~Rs.16 Bn/yr. Before Lahore North, fixing only the southern AC lines would mean the AC corridor could carry 3,200–3,600 MW, but the HVDC is still capped at 2,700 MW — a total ceiling of ~4,500–4,600 MW in theory, but with no improvement in HVDC utilisation. The prioritisation is clear: unlock 1,300 MW at the northern end (P1), then remove the 450 MW AC constraint at the southern end (P3).

The 74% Solution

Lahore North Grid Station: How One Substation Resolves 1,300 MW of Congestion

The core mechanism: The ±660 kV HVDC uses Line-Commutated Converter (LCC) technology at both terminals. LCC converters require a strong AC voltage source to commutate — they cannot operate in isolation from the AC network. At the Lahore end, the existing AC network (Sheikhupura, Gatti, Yousafwala substations) can absorb approximately 2,700 MW of DC power under N-1 conditions before voltage drops and commutation failure risk become unacceptable. Lahore North adds a new 500 kV grid station with its associated transformation and sub-transmission lines, raising the Lahore AC network's absorption capacity to support the full 4,000 MW HVDC rating under N-1. The 1,300 MW gain is the direct arithmetic difference between the N-1-limited 2,700 MW ceiling today and the 4,000 MW rated capacity after commissioning.

The theoretical 1,300 MW capacity gain does not translate uniformly across all months. In winter, northern hydro, gas, and Chashma plants cover more of the load — the HVDC runs below its pre-Lahore-North ceiling for demand reasons, not infrastructure reasons. The gain concentrates overwhelmingly in summer peak months (June–October), which is precisely when Pakistan's electricity crisis is most acute.

Economic Recalibration

How Chashma C5 and BTM Solar Change — and Don't Change — the Investment Case

Two structural changes — Chashma C5 adding ~1,200 MW of new northern nuclear generation, and BTM solar growing at 2–3 GW-DC per year — affect the economics of some TIP projects. They do not affect the case for completing Lahore North. They do reduce the marginal value of net-new 500 kV AC corridors beyond what is needed to complement the existing HVDC, and they require P4 projects to be re-modelled before construction commitment.

Chashma C5

A Northern Plant That Does Not Use the Corridor — But Reduces Its Marginal Value

Chashma C1–C4 (1,330 MW combined) sits at Mianwali — northern Punjab, on the KPK border. In FY2024-25, C1 operated at 82.3% CUF, C2 at 86.3%, C3 at 86.2%, and C4 at 91.7% — the highest utilisation of any thermal or nuclear units in Pakistan. C5 will be at the same site at ~1,200 MW and a projected 90%+ CUF, generating approximately 9.4 TWh/year. That output serves Punjab load directly. It does not cross the North–South corridor at all.

The corridor investment case rests on the marginal cost differential between what the south can supply (coal/nuclear at Rs.10–14/kWh all-in) and what the north currently has to burn when the corridor is constrained (RLNG at Rs.18–22/kWh). C5 displaces approximately 1,000–1,200 MW of that northern RLNG demand permanently — reducing the hours during which the corridor's differential is realised, and therefore reducing the dispatch substitution saving by approximately Rs.18–25 Bn/yr on a steady-state basis.

But the HVDC sunk cost argument is unaffected. Rs.97.8 billion flows to PMLTC whether the HVDC carries 1,400 MW or 4,000 MW. C5 does not reduce this cost by a single rupee. The only lever that changes the effective per-unit HVDC burden is pushing the line harder — which requires Lahore North. This logic holds unconditionally.

BTM Solar

The Duck Curve Strengthens the Evening HVDC Case, Not Weakens It

26.8 GW of BTM solar is installed and growing at 2–3 GW-DC per year. The duck curve effect — suppressed grid demand during solar hours (09:00–15:00), steep ramp in the evening (17:00–21:00) — is already visible in ISMO load data. At first glance this appears to reduce the corridor's value: if daytime grid demand falls, the corridor carries less midday power. The conclusion is wrong for two reasons.

First, BTM suppresses grid demand specifically during the 2,500–2,900 solar hours per year. The remaining 5,900–6,300 hours — evenings, nights, early mornings — remain entirely grid-dependent. Peak demand in the evening still reaches 14,000–16,000 MW. The corridor is needed for every non-solar hour, and those hours are the hours when Pakistani consumers actually face load-shedding and highest prices.

Second, BTM makes the corridor more valuable for baseload plants, not less. As daytime solar suppresses the midday system marginal price to Rs.2–5/kWh, the only plants economical enough to run into this price floor are nuclear and coal — exactly the plants in the South. Those plants stay online through the solar day, and when the evening ramp hits and 10,000–12,000 MW of BTM solar goes dark, they are positioned to serve the evening peak — but only if the corridor can carry them north. Without the corridor, ISMO is forced to keep RLNG running all day at part-load just to be available for 17:00–21:00. The corridor enables baseload to cover the flat portion, and RLNG to cover only the steep ramp it is designed for.

BTM Effect on Corridor Value: Day Demand Profile (Illustrative)

Illustrative, based on ISMO marginal cost vs. demand data. BTM suppresses midday grid load (reducing corridor daytime throughput) but steepens the evening ramp — when southern baseload dispatched via HVDC is most valuable for avoiding RLNG peaking.

The P4 re-modelling implication: Net-new 500 kV AC corridors designed for peak southward transfer must model the reduced daytime throughput from BTM suppression. A corridor sized for 2022 demand assumptions may over-provision daytime capacity while still under-provisioning evening baseload dispatch capacity. The answer may be smarter grid management (DLR, storage) rather than more raw wires.

Renewable Evacuation

Wind Curtailment: A Separate Problem With a Separate Fix

519 GWh of zero-marginal-cost wind was physically curtailed in FY2024-25, generating Rs.12.8 billion in NPMV payments. This problem is structurally independent of the North–South corridor: the Matiari 500 kV hub (the HVDC anchor) is rated at 4,000 MW against peak wind dispatch of only ~900 MW — the HVDC is not the constraint. The constraint is the 220 kV AC sub-transmission connecting wind farms to Matiari, and it affects zero projects discussed in the N-S corridor section. The fix is local, fast, and has a sub-one-year payback.

Lahore North alone (Rs.35–50 Bn capex already spent): The final 4% of construction to energisation costs a fraction of the Rs.45 Bn/yr benefit. Even on the conservative C5-adjusted dispatch saving of Rs.20 Bn/yr, the marginal capex to completion has an effective payback of weeks, not years. This is the most capital-efficient action in the entire Pakistan power sector.

On P4 payback: Net-new 500 kV AC corridors that presuppose demand growth beyond current HVDC capacity now have payback periods extending beyond 15 years under C5 + BTM assumptions. This is the quantitative basis for "refresh economics before committing" — not cancellation, but disciplined sequencing.

Reform Sequencing

Three-Phase Roadmap — Sequenced by Priority

The roadmap below directly reflects the priority stack. Phase 1 is dominated by completing what is already built (Lahore North) and the zero-capex regulatory action (ISMO reform). Phase 2 addresses the wind bottleneck and reinforces the corridor. Phase 3 completes the N-S AC constraint removal and builds the market architecture that makes BTM an asset rather than a threat.

Phase 1 — 2025–2026: Complete What's Built, Reform What's Free
Q3 2025 — P1 CRITICAL
★ Lahore North 500 kV GS — Full Commissioning
Energisation and stability testing. HVDC loading moves toward 3,000–4,000 MW in summer months. Constraint A resolved (74%, 1,300 MW). Annual dispatch saving: Rs.33–56 Bn. HVDC per-unit cost falls from Rs.7.39 → Rs.4.30/kWh effective.
Q3–Q4 2025 — P2 Zero-Capex
ISMO Merit-Order Curtailment Reform
NEPRA directive requiring Cost-Plus wind plants (Rs.18.22/kWh NPMV) to be curtailed before Upfront plants (Rs.34.68/kWh NPMV). Rs.3.8 Bn annual saving. Single operational protocol change — no infrastructure, no renegotiation.
Q4 2025 – Q2 2026 — P2
Jamshoro 500/220 kV Transformer Augmentation
Removes the single-point-of-failure risk at the 10,645 MW southern hub. Enables full N-1-compliant dispatch of Port Qasim and Hub — reinforcing the Lahore North gain at the southern end of the same dispatch chain.
Q2 2026 — P2
Jhimpir 220 kV GIS Expansion (900 → 1,400 MW)
Eliminates seasonal wind NPMV peaks. Rs.8–10 Bn/yr saving. Capex: USD 35–50 Mn. Sub-one-year payback.
Phase 2 — 2026–2027: Southern Corridor + Wind 2nd Corridor
Q1–Q2 2026 — P3 (begins)
Dadu–Shikarpur 500 kV Upgrade
First of three southern AC line upgrades comprising Constraint B. Removes approximately 200 MW of residual curtailment. Land acquisition under way — contracting must begin immediately to hit this window.
Q3 2026 – Q2 2027 — P3
Shikarpur–Guddu + Moro–RYK 500 kV Upgrades
Completes Constraint B removal. Residual ~450 MW of curtailment eliminated. Total N-S congestion: near zero. Combined P3 saving: Rs.16 Bn/yr. Note: slightly reduced vs. original estimate due to C5 effect.
Q3 2026 – Q4 2027 — P2
Jhimpir–Dhabeji 2nd Wind Evacuation Corridor (220 kV)
Structural elimination of Jhimpir single-point-of-failure. Direct access from Wave-3 wind plants to Matiari 500 kV hub. Capex: USD 80–120 Mn. Eliminates residual NPMV risk.
Q2–Q4 2026
Reactive Power Market — Phase 1 (NEPRA Act §23H(c))
Pakistan's first ancillary services market. Creates price signal for voltage support; incentivises STATCOM investment at Matiari and Lahore converters. Enables higher HVDC loading without voltage instability.
